Intro to Category Theory · Lesson 04

Universal Properties

Three lessons of arrow-pushing pay off: we now define objects by how everything else maps to them — and the definitions pin their subjects down up to unique isomorphism.

§1 — The payoff

Defining things by the company they keep

So far we have described objects from the outside — by their arrows — but we have not yet defined anything that way. This lesson makes the move. A universal property characterizes an object as the best possible solution to a mapping problem: the object such that every other candidate factors through it, in exactly one way.

The template has two clauses, and both matter. Existence: for every candidate, some arrow does the job. Uniqueness: only one arrow does. Existence alone would give a “good enough” object; uniqueness is what makes it the object, canonical, with no arbitrary choices hiding inside. In the diagram above, the dashed amber arrow is this lesson’s signature mark: there exists exactly one arrow making the picture commute.

The reward for this austerity is the theorem we prove in §2: anything defined by a universal property is unique up to unique isomorphism. Two constructions built in wildly different ways — cartesian pairs versus some exotic encoding — are interchangeable the moment they satisfy the same universal property. Interfaces over implementations, stated as mathematics.

§2 — Terminal & initial objects

The simplest universal properties there are

Definition · Terminal and initial
  • An object T is terminal if for every object X there is exactly one arrow X → T. It is written 1, and the unique arrow !X : X → 1.
  • An object I is initial if for every object X there is exactly one arrow I → X. It is written 0.

In Set, a one-element set {∗} is terminal: a function into it has no choices to make, so there is exactly one from each X. The empty set is initial: the empty function is the unique map ∅ → X — there is nothing to send, hence nothing to choose, hence exactly one way to do it. (Vacuous, and correct.) Note the asymmetry: Set has many terminal objects — every singleton — which is exactly why the uniqueness theorem below matters. In a preorder, terminal means greatest element and initial means least; in Grp, the trivial group is both at once (a zero object).

Theorem · Unique up to unique isomorphism

If T and T′ are both terminal in 𝒞, there is exactly one arrow T → T′, and it is an isomorphism.

Proof (arrows only). Terminality of T′ gives a unique arrow u : T → T′; terminality of T gives a unique v : T′ → T. Now consider v ∘ u : T → T. Since T is terminal, there is exactly one arrow T → T — and idT is such an arrow. So v ∘ u = idT, and symmetrically u ∘ v = idT′. Hence u is an isomorphism, and it was unique from the start. ∎

Read the proof again — it is four sentences, and the engine of all four is the word exactly. This one argument, lightly costumed, will re-prove itself for products below, for limits in Lesson 5, and for adjoints after that. It is the most reusable proof in the subject.

§3 — Products

The pair, without ever saying “pair”

What is the cartesian product A × B, arrows-only? Its interface: it comes with two projections, and knowing a map into A × B is exactly the same as knowing a map into A and a map into B. Promote the interface to the definition:

Definition · Product

A product of A and B is an object A × B together with arrows π1 : A × B → A and π2 : A × B → B (the projections) such that: for every object X and every pair of arrows f : X → A, g : X → B, there is exactly one arrow ⟨f, g⟩ : X → A × B with

π1 ∘ ⟨f, g⟩ = f   and   π2 ∘ ⟨f, g⟩ = g.

That is the hero diagram at the top of the page: the solid arrows are the given data, and the dashed ⟨f, g⟩ is the one arrow the universal property conjures. In Set it is x ↦ (f(x), g(x)) — and the uniqueness clause holds because any map m with π1 ∘ m = f and π2 ∘ m = g has both coordinates of m(x) pinned down.

Now the definition goes traveling. In Grp, the same property picks out the direct product of groups. In a preorder, an arrow X → A just means X ≤ A, so the product of A and B is the greatest element below both — the meet. In the divisibility order on positive integers, “below” means “divides,” and the product of two numbers is their gcd. One definition; pairs, direct products, meets, and gcds fall out as instances. And by the same style of argument as §2’s theorem, any two products of A and B are uniquely isomorphic (Exercise 5) — so we may speak of the product.

A warning with content: products need not exist. The definition is a specification, not a guarantee, and some categories simply have no object meeting it — §5 exhibits one and proves it by exhaustive search. Whether a category has products is real information about the category.

§4 — Coproducts & duality

Flip every arrow, get a second theory free

Lesson 1’s Exercise 7 built the opposite category 𝒞op: same objects, every arrow reversed. Here is why that construction earns its keep. Any arrows-only definition can be interpreted in 𝒞op instead of 𝒞, which reverses every arrow in the definition — producing a dual concept in 𝒞 itself. Initial is dual to terminal. And dual to the product is:

Definition · Coproduct (the product, arrows reversed)

A coproduct of A and B is an object A + B with arrows ι1 : A → A + B and ι2 : B → A + B (the injections) such that for every X and every f : A → X, g : B → X, there is exactly one [f, g] : A + B → X with [f, g] ∘ ι1 = f and [f, g] ∘ ι2 = g.

In Set, the coproduct is the disjoint union: to define a function out of A ⊔ B you say what happens on the A-part and what happens on the B-part, and [f, g] is exactly that case split — in code, pattern-matching on a tagged union or Either type. In a preorder it is the join (least upper bound); in divisibility, the lcm. In Grp it is the free product — famously not the direct product, a reminder that duals can look very different from their originals even when the definitions are mirror images.

The duality principle

Every theorem proved from the category axioms alone dualizes: reverse all arrows in both the statement and the proof, and the result is again a theorem, because the axioms themselves are self-dual (Lesson 1, Exercise 7). So §2’s uniqueness theorem for terminal objects is automatically also a uniqueness theorem for initial objects, and the uniqueness of products yields uniqueness of coproducts — no new work. One proof, two theorems, forever. This is the single best bargain in mathematics.

§5 — Universal properties in Python

A universal property is a search problem — so search

In a finite category, “there exists exactly one arrow such that…” is checkable by enumeration. That makes every definition in this lesson executable. We write finders for terminal objects, initial objects, and products, and point them at the divisibility poset from §3. (These build on the FiniteCategory class from Lesson 1; the downloadable script is self-contained.)

universal.py — finders for universal objects
def arrows_between(C, X, Y):
    return [a for a, (d, c) in C.ar.items() if d == X and c == Y]

def terminal_objects(C):
    "T is terminal iff every object has EXACTLY ONE arrow into T."
    return [T for T in C.obj
            if all(len(arrows_between(C, X, T)) == 1 for X in C.obj)]

def initial_objects(C):
    return [I for I in C.obj
            if all(len(arrows_between(C, I, X)) == 1 for X in C.obj)]

def is_product(C, P, pA, pB):
    "Check the universal property of (P, pA: P->A, pB: P->B)."
    A, B = C.cod(pA), C.cod(pB)
    for X in C.obj:
        for f in arrows_between(C, X, A):
            for g in arrows_between(C, X, B):
                mediators = [m for m in arrows_between(C, X, P)
                             if C.comp[(pA, m)] == f and C.comp[(pB, m)] == g]
                if len(mediators) != 1:      # exists AND is unique
                    return False
    return True

def find_products(C, A, B):
    return [(P, pA, pB) for P in C.obj
            for pA in arrows_between(C, P, A)
            for pB in arrows_between(C, P, B)
            if is_product(C, P, pA, pB)]

# The divisibility order on {1, 2, 3, 6}:  arrow a -> b  iff  a divides b
objs = {1, 2, 3, 6}
ar   = {f"{a}|{b}": (a, b) for a in objs for b in objs if b % a == 0}
comp = {}
for g, (gs, gt) in ar.items():
    for f, (fs, ft) in ar.items():
        if ft == gs:
            comp[(g, f)] = f"{fs}|{gt}"      # thin category: forced
ident = {n: f"{n}|{n}" for n in objs}
Div = FiniteCategory(objs, ar, comp, ident)

print("initial: ", initial_objects(Div))
print("terminal:", terminal_objects(Div))
print("2 x 3 =", find_products(Div, 2, 3))   # expect gcd = 1
print("2 x 6 =", find_products(Div, 2, 6))   # expect gcd = 2
outputinitial: [1] terminal: [6] 2 x 3 = [(1, '1|2', '1|3')] 2 x 6 = [(2, '2|2', '2|6')]

The finder confirms §3’s claims: in the divisibility order, initial is 1, terminal is 6, and products compute gcds. Now the promised non-existence result. In the one-object category Z3 (our running monoid), a product of ∗ with ∗ would need projections p, q such that every pair (f, g) factors uniquely — and no choice of p, q manages it. The search space is tiny, so exhaustion is a proof:

no_products.py — a category with no products
# Z3 = one object '*', arrows {0,1,2}, composition = addition mod 3
zar  = {n: ('*', '*') for n in range(3)}
zcmp = {(a, b): (a + b) % 3 for a in range(3) for b in range(3)}
Z3   = FiniteCategory({'*'}, zar, zcmp, {'*': 0})

print(find_products(Z3, '*', '*'))    # exhaustive search over all
                                      # candidate (P, p, q) triples
output[]

Why it fails is worth seeing by hand: a mediator m for the pair (f, g) must satisfy p + m = f and q + m = g in ℤ/3, forcing m = f − p = g − q — possible only when f − g = p − q, which fails for most pairs. Existence breaks, not just uniqueness. Finally, the product in Set itself, with its mediating map as a working function:

pairing.py — the product in Set
def pairing(f, g):
    "the unique mediating map  <f, g> : X -> A x B"
    return lambda x: (f(x), g(x))

fst = lambda p: p[0]
snd = lambda p: p[1]

X = [0, 1, 2]
f = lambda x: x + 10          # f : X -> A
g = lambda x: x * x           # g : X -> B
m = pairing(f, g)

# both triangles of the universal property commute:
assert all(fst(m(x)) == f(x) for x in X)
assert all(snd(m(x)) == g(x) for x in X)
print([m(x) for x in X])
output[(10, 0), (11, 1), (12, 4)]
Going deeper

Elements, generalized

Terminal objects quietly repair the ban on elements. In Set, an arrow 1 → X from the one-point set is exactly a choice of one element of X — so “element” can be defined arrows-only: a global element of X is an arrow 1 → X from the terminal object. In Set this recovers everything: functions are determined by what they do to global elements.

In most categories, though, global elements are scarce — Exercise 9 exhibits a poset where only one object has any — and functions are not determined by them. The repair is to widen the probe: an arrow U → X from an arbitrary object is a generalized element of X “of shape U.” Probing with all shapes at once recovers everything in every category — that is the course’s slogan, and Lesson 7’s Yoneda lemma is precisely the theorem that the full dossier of generalized elements determines the object. This little section is the bridge between the slogan’s two banks.

§6 — Exercises

Ten problems on universal properties

These lean on the uniqueness clause more than any previous set — whenever you are stuck, ask “what is the only arrow that could possibly work here?” and let exactly one do the rest.

Exercise 1warm-up

Work in Set. For a set X with 5 elements:

  1. How many functions are there from X to the one-element set {∗}? What does your answer confirm?
  2. How many functions are there from the empty set ∅ to X? And how many from X to ∅? What do these two answers say about ∅?
Show solution

(a) Exactly one. A function must assign each of the 5 elements a value in {∗}, and there is only one value available — no choices anywhere. This confirms singletons are terminal.

(b) From ∅ to X: exactly one — the empty function. There is nothing to assign, hence exactly one way to complete the (empty) assignment. From X to ∅: zero — element x would need a value in ∅, and there are none. So ∅ is initial but not terminal: Set has no zero object, unlike Grp. The count “one map out, no maps in” is the entire personality of the empty set, expressed in arrows.

Exercise 2arrows-only

State and prove the dual of §2’s theorem: any two initial objects are isomorphic via a unique isomorphism.

Write the proof out in full — then compare it, line by line, with the terminal-object proof, and mark where each arrow reversed.

Show solution

Let I and I′ be initial. Initiality of I gives a unique u : I → I′; initiality of I′ gives a unique v : I′ → I. The composite v ∘ u : I → I is an arrow out of the initial I, of which there is exactly one — and idI qualifies. So v ∘ u = idI, and symmetrically u ∘ v = idI′: a unique isomorphism.

Comparing: every “arrow into” became “arrow out of,” every use of terminality became initiality, and the composite v ∘ u was pinned down as an endomorphism of I rather than T. Nothing else changed. That mechanical translation is the duality principle in action — you could have skipped the proof entirely and cited §4’s amber box, but it is worth doing by hand exactly once.

Exercise 3structure

Work in an arbitrary preorder, viewed as a category.

  1. Unwind the definitions: what do “terminal object” and “initial object” mean in order language?
  2. Unwind the product: show that a product of a and b is exactly a meet (greatest lower bound), and note which part of the universal property becomes which clause of “greatest lower bound.”
Show solution

(a) Terminal: an object with an arrow from everything — i.e. an element ⊤ with x ≤ ⊤ for all x: a greatest element. Dually, initial is a least element. (Uniqueness of arrows is free — the category is thin.)

(b) The projections a × b → a and a × b → b say the product is a lower bound: a × b ≤ a and a × b ≤ b. The mediating-arrow clause says every x with x ≤ a and x ≤ b satisfies x ≤ a × b — the product is greatest among lower bounds. Existence of the mediator is the “greatest” clause; its uniqueness is free by thinness. So products in a preorder are meets, and dually coproducts are joins — and in the divisibility order these are gcd and lcm, as §5 verified by machine.

Exercise 4examples

A zero object is one that is initial and terminal at once. §2 noted that the trivial group is a zero object in Grp.

  1. Verify both halves: for any group G, count the homomorphisms {e} → G and G → {e}.
  2. Explain why the analogous count fails in Set, even though Set has both an initial and a terminal object.
Show solution

(a) A homomorphism must send identity to identity. Out of {e}: the only element must go to the identity of G, so exactly one homomorphism — {e} is initial. Into {e}: every element has nowhere to go but e, so again exactly one — terminal too. One object, both properties: a zero object.

(b) In Set the initial object is ∅ and the terminal objects are singletons, and no set is both: ∅ ≇ {∗} (there is no arrow {∗} → ∅ at all, per Exercise 1). The difference-maker is the group identity: it gives every hom-set a guaranteed inhabitant, collapsing “empty” and “one-point” into the same object. Whether a category has a zero object is a coarse but genuinely useful invariant — it is why “the zero map” makes sense between any two groups but not between any two sets.

Exercise 5arrows-only

Adapt §2’s uniqueness proof to products. Suppose (P, π1, π2) and (P′, π′1, π′2) are both products of A and B. Show there is a unique isomorphism u : P → P′ that is compatible with the projections, i.e. π′1 ∘ u = π1 and π′2 ∘ u = π2.

Hint: to show a certain composite equals an identity, ask what the uniqueness clause says about arrows P → P that commute with both projections.

Show solution

The universal property of P′, applied to the arrows π1, π2 out of P, produces a unique u = ⟨π1, π2⟩′ : P → P′ compatible with the projections. Symmetrically, P’s universal property produces v : P′ → P with πi ∘ v = π′i.

Now the punchline. The composite v ∘ u : P → P commutes with both projections: πi ∘ (v ∘ u) = π′i ∘ u = πi. But P’s universal property, applied to the pair (π1, π2) itself, says there is exactly one arrow P → P commuting with both projections — and idP visibly does. Therefore v ∘ u = idP, and symmetrically u ∘ v = idP′. Same skeleton as §2: manufacture arrows with existence, collapse composites with uniqueness. Once you have written this proof twice you will never need to write it again — Lesson 5 states it once for all limits.

Exercise 6structure

In Set, take A = {1, 2} and B = {2, 3}, and let X = {'lo', 'hi'}. Define f : A → X by 1, 2 ↦ 'lo' and g : B → X by 2 ↦ 'lo', 3 ↦ 'hi'.

  1. Describe the coproduct A + B concretely. Why must the two copies of the element 2 be kept apart?
  2. Write out the copairing [f, g] : A + B → X explicitly, and check both triangle identities.
Show solution

(a) The coproduct is the disjoint union — implement it with tags: A + B = {('L',1), ('L',2), ('R',2), ('R',3)}, with injections ι1(a) = ('L', a) and ι2(b) = ('R', b). The two 2’s must stay distinct because the universal property demands that f and g be recoverable independently: had we merged the 2’s, a pair with f(2) ≠ g(2) would have no well-defined copairing at all. (Here f(2) = g(2) = 'lo' by luck, but the coproduct must work for every pair.)

(b) [f, g] pattern-matches on the tag: ('L',1) ↦ 'lo', ('L',2) ↦ 'lo', ('R',2) ↦ 'lo', ('R',3) ↦ 'hi'. The triangles: [f,g](ι1(a)) = f(a) for both a, and [f,g](ι2(b)) = g(b) for both b — check each of the four elements. This is exactly the match statement on an Either/tagged-union type; the coproduct’s universal property is the mathematical spec of case analysis.

Exercise 7think

§5 showed by exhaustive search that the one-object category Z3 has no binary products. Now prove it by hand, for the general one-object category built from a group (G, +) with more than one element.

Suppose (∗, p, q) were a product of ∗ with ∗. Analyze what a mediating arrow for a pair (f, g) would have to be, and derive a contradiction.

Show solution

A mediator for the pair (f, g) is an element m ∈ G with p + m = f and q + m = g. In a group each equation has exactly one solution: m = −p + f from the first and m = −q + g from the second. A mediator exists iff these agree, i.e. iff −p + f = −q + g, i.e. iff g = q − p + f.

But the universal property requires a mediator for every pair (f, g) ∈ G × G — and the condition pins g to a single value once f is chosen. Since |G| > 1, choose any g′ ≠ q − p + f: the pair (f, g′) has no mediator. Existence fails, so no (p, q) works, so there is no product. Intuition to keep: a product must be able to store two independent pieces of information, and a single object whose arrows form a group has no room — every equation is exactly solvable, leaving no slack for a second coordinate.

Exercise 8code

Write find_coproducts(C, A, B) by dualizing find_products from §5 — reverse the direction of the candidate injections and of the mediating arrow, and flip the order of each composition accordingly.

Run it on the divisibility poset to confirm that the coproduct of 2 and 3 is 6 (the lcm). Then run it on Z3 and interpret the result in the light of duality.

Show solution

Every arrows_between call flips its arguments, and each composite comp[(proj, m)] becomes comp[(m, inj)] — the mediator is now applied after the injection:

coproducts.py
def find_coproducts(C, A, B):
    hits = []
    for P in C.obj:
        for iA in arrows_between(C, A, P):
            for iB in arrows_between(C, B, P):
                ok = True
                for X in C.obj:
                    for f in arrows_between(C, A, X):
                        for g in arrows_between(C, B, X):
                            med = [m for m in arrows_between(C, P, X)
                                   if C.comp[(m, iA)] == f
                                   and C.comp[(m, iB)] == g]
                            if len(med) != 1: ok = False
                if ok: hits.append((P, iA, iB))
    return hits

print(find_coproducts(Div, 2, 3))   # [(6, '2|6', '3|6')]  -- the lcm
print(find_coproducts(Z3, '*', '*'))  # []
output[(6, '2|6', '3|6')] []

Z3 has no coproducts either — as duality predicts. Z3 is isomorphic to its own opposite (reverse the arrows of a commutative group and relabel), so “no products in Z3op” translates directly into “no coproducts in Z3.” Exercise 7’s by-hand proof dualizes the same way, word for word.

Exercise 9structure · new

Global elements, counted in two categories:

  1. In Set, exhibit the bijection between arrows 1 → X and elements of X, and explain why two functions f, g : X → Y agreeing on all global elements must be equal.
  2. In the divisibility poset {1, 2, 3, 6} (terminal object: 6), which objects have a global element? What does the answer say about the reach of global elements outside Set?
Show solution

(a) A function from a one-point set has exactly one value to choose, so arrows 1 → X correspond to elements x ∈ X via ∗ ↦ x. If f, g agree on every global element, then for each x, composing with the arrow picking x gives f(x) = g(x) — pointwise agreement, hence equality. Set is well-pointed: the single probe 1 suffices.

(b) A global element of x is an arrow 6 → x, which exists iff 6 | x — so only x = 6 has one. Every other object is invisible to the probe 1, and any two monotone maps out of this poset trivially “agree on global elements.” One probe is nowhere near enough in general; you need them all, which is exactly the widening that generalized elements — and, in Lesson 7, the Yoneda embedding — perform.

Exercise 10arrows-only · new

Products associate, up to the usual unique isomorphism. Suppose the products (A × B) × C and A × (B × C) both exist.

Equip (A × B) × C with three “projections” to A, B, and C, and show it satisfies the universal property of a triple product: for any f : X → A, g : X → B, h : X → C, exactly one arrow into it commutes with all three. Conclude — without further computation — that (A × B) × C ≅ A × (B × C).

Show solution

The three projections: π1 ∘ π1, π2 ∘ π1, and π2 (outer). Given f, g, h, build the mediator in two stages: pair f and g into ⟨f, g⟩ : X → A × B, then pair with h: ⟨⟨f, g⟩, h⟩. The three triangles commute by unwinding the two pairings.

Uniqueness stacks the two uniqueness clauses: a competitor m commuting with all three projections has π1 ∘ m commuting with both inner projections, so π1 ∘ m = ⟨f, g⟩ by the inner product’s uniqueness — and then m = ⟨⟨f, g⟩, h⟩ by the outer’s. So (A × B) × C is a triple product; by the mirror argument so is A × (B × C); and §2’s theorem (two objects with the same universal property) supplies the unique isomorphism, no components ever computed. The same template gives commutativity A × B ≅ B × A and, in Lesson 5’s language, both are limits over the same three-dot diagram — associativity is then not even a theorem, just two names for one limit.

Recap

What to carry forward

  1. A universal property defines an object by a mapping problem it solves best: for every candidate, exactly one arrow factors through it. Existence and uniqueness are separate clauses, and both carry weight.
  2. Terminal and initial objects are the smallest instances; anything universal is unique up to unique isomorphism, by a four-sentence proof that recurs throughout the subject.
  3. The product is the pair defined without elements — and the same definition yields direct products, meets, and gcds, depending on the category. Products need not exist, and whether they do is real information.
  4. Duality: reverse all arrows and every definition and theorem dualizes — coproducts (disjoint unions, joins, lcms, tagged unions) come free from products.
  5. In a finite category, a universal property is a finite search, so all of this is executable — including non-existence proofs by exhaustion.
