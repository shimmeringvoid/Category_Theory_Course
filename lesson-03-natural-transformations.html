Intro to Category Theory · Lesson 03

Natural Transformations

A way to slide one functor onto another, one arrow per object, so coherently that it commutes with everything. This is the idea the whole field was invented to make precise.

§1 — One more floor up

The maps between functors

Lesson 1 gave us categories. Lesson 2 gave us functors — the maps between categories — and noticed they bundle into a category, Cat. The reflex of the subject now fires one more time. Fix two categories 𝒞 and 𝒟; there are many functors between them. What is the right notion of a map between two functors?

This is not an afterthought — it is the reason category theory exists. Eilenberg and Mac Lane kept saying things like “there is a natural map from a space to its double dual,” and wanted to pin down what “natural” meant, as opposed to a map that merely happens to exist once you make arbitrary choices. The slogan they left behind: categories were defined in order to define functors, and functors were defined in order to define natural transformations.

The friendliest first example lives in code. A polymorphic function like reverse works at every element type at once and never looks at the elements themselves — only at the structure holding them. That uniformity, made precise, is exactly naturality. By the end of this lesson you’ll be checking it with an assertion.

§2 — The definition

A coherent family of arrows, one per object

Suppose F and G are both functors 𝒞 → 𝒟 — same source, same target. Think of them as two ways of drawing a picture of 𝒞 inside 𝒟. A natural transformation is a way to slide the first picture onto the second.

Definition · Natural transformation

A natural transformation α : F ⇒ G assigns:

  • to each object X of 𝒞, an arrow αX : F X → G X in 𝒟 — its component at X

such that for every arrow f : X → Y in 𝒞, the naturality square commutes:

NaturalityG f ∘ αX = αY ∘ F f

Read the square at the top of the page: the two paths from F X to G Y — go across by αX then down by G f, or down by F f then across by αY — must agree. The single equation has to hold for every arrow of 𝒞 at once. That is what stops α from being an arbitrary pile of arrows: each component is pinned to its neighbours by the action of every morphism. There is no freedom to wire things up inconsistently.

§3 — Naturality in the wild

Polymorphism, determinants, and the double dual

The cleanest examples are functions you already trust. Take F = G = List and let the component be reverse. The naturality square says: for any f, mapping f after reversing equals reversing after mapping f. It holds for a structural reason — reverse only rearranges positions, it never inspects the elements, so it cannot matter whether you transform the elements before or after.

This is not a coincidence of reverse. A function whose type is genuinely polymorphic — defined uniformly for all types, blind to the contents — is forced to be natural; the naturality square comes packaged with the type signature. (This is the heart of what’s called a “free theorem.”) The same goes for safe_head : List ⇒ Maybe, concat, length seen as List ⇒ Const ℕ, and so on.

Aside · What “natural” was invented to mean

For every vector space V there is a map to its double dual V → V** sending a vector to “evaluation at that vector.” It needs no choice of basis, and it assembles into a natural transformation from the identity functor to the double-dual functor. The single dual V → V* is different: an isomorphism exists for finite-dimensional V, but only after choosing a basis, and it is not natural. The gap between “isomorphic” and “naturally isomorphic” is precisely the gap these definitions were built to capture. In mainstream mathematics the determinant is another: det is a natural transformation from GLn to the units functor, commuting with every ring homomorphism.

When every component αX is an isomorphism, α is a natural isomorphism, written F ≅ G. This is the correct sense in which two functors can be “the same.”

Lemma · Inverses are automatically natural

If α : F ⇒ G has every component invertible, the inverse components (αX)−1 assemble into a natural transformation G ⇒ F. So “natural iso” needs only that each αX be an iso — coherence of the inverse comes for free.

Proof (arrows only). Start from the naturality square G f ∘ αX = αY ∘ F f. Pre-compose with (αX)−1 and post-compose with (αY)−1; the components cancel by the inverse laws and you are left with (αY)−1 ∘ G f = F f ∘ (αX)−1, which is exactly the naturality square for the inverse family. ∎

§4 — The functor category

Natural transformations compose, so they too form a category

Given α : F ⇒ G and β : G ⇒ H, define their composite component-wise: (β ∘ α)X = βX ∘ αX, composing inside 𝒟. Is it natural? Draw the two naturality squares side by side, sharing the middle edge G f:

The identity natural transformation 1F has components idF X, and this composition is associative because composition in 𝒟 is. Recognize the pattern? It is the Lesson 1 definition of a category yet again. Fixing 𝒞 and 𝒟, the functors 𝒞 → 𝒟 are the objects and natural transformations are the arrows — a category, the functor category [𝒞, 𝒟].

And the loop closes neatly: an isomorphism in [𝒞, 𝒟] — in the Lesson 1 sense of an arrow with a two-sided inverse — is exactly a natural isomorphism, which by the lemma above is exactly a natural transformation whose components are all isos. Three vantage points, one notion.

One level higher, in passing

There is a second way to compose — horizontally, across different pairs of categories — and the two compositions satisfy an interchange law. That structure makes Cat a 2-category: objects, arrows between them, and arrows between those. We won’t need the machinery here; it’s enough to notice the tower keeps going up.

§5 — Naturality in Python

Check a square, then check it for a finite functor

First, the structural examples. We confirm the naturality square for reverse (a transformation List ⇒ List) and for safe_head (a transformation List ⇒ Maybe, with Maybe modelled as a list of length 0 or 1).

natural.py — polymorphic functions are natural
def fmap(f, xs):                 # the List functor on arrows
    return [f(x) for x in xs]

def reverse(xs):                 # the component of  reverse : List => List
    return xs[::-1]

inc  = lambda x: x + 1
data = [1, 2, 3, 4]

# Naturality of reverse:   map f . reverse  ==  reverse . map f
assert fmap(inc, reverse(data)) == reverse(fmap(inc, data))
print(fmap(inc, reverse(data)))              # [5, 4, 3, 2]

# safe_head : List => Maybe   (Maybe modelled as a 0-or-1 element list)
def safe_head(xs):
    return [xs[0]] if xs else []

# Naturality of safe_head holds for every list and every f
for xs in ([], [10, 20, 30]):
    assert fmap(inc, safe_head(xs)) == safe_head(fmap(inc, xs))
print("reverse and safe_head are natural")
output[5, 4, 3, 2] reverse and safe_head are natural

Now the finite version, reusing FiniteCategory and Functor from the previous lessons. A natural transformation is one component arrow per object; check() verifies the typing and the naturality square for every arrow. We use the cleanest possible illustration: two functors out of 𝟚 pick two opposite edges of a commuting square, and a natural transformation between them is that commuting square — its components are the other two edges.

nat_transformation.py — a naturality checker
class Nat:
    """alpha : F => G, with F, G : C -> D sharing source and target.
       `comp` maps each object X of C to a component arrow alpha_X of D."""
    def __init__(self, F, G, comp):
        self.F, self.G, self.comp = F, G, comp

    def check(self):
        F, G, C, D = self.F, self.G, self.F.C, self.F.D
        for X in C.obj:           # component typing:  alpha_X : F X -> G X
            assert D.ar[self.comp[X]] == (F.on_obj[X], G.on_obj[X])
        for f in C.ar:            # naturality:  G f . alpha_X == alpha_Y . F f
            X, Y = C.ar[f]
            assert D.comp[(G.on_arr[f], self.comp[X])] == \
                   D.comp[(self.comp[Y], F.on_arr[f])]
        return True

# F and G send the category 2 to two opposite edges of the square; a natural
# transformation between them is EXACTLY a commuting square (components p, q).
F = Functor(Two, Square, {0:'A', 1:'B'}, {'i0':'iA', 'i1':'iB', 'u':'f'})
G = Functor(Two, Square, {0:'C', 1:'D'}, {'i0':'iC', 'i1':'iD', 'u':'g'})
alpha = Nat(F, G, comp={0:'p', 1:'q'})
print(alpha.check())
outputTrue

The downloadable script builds Square (objects A, B, C, D with q ∘ f = g ∘ p) and Two in full. The lesson made true a slogan worth keeping: a natural transformation between two arrow-shaped diagrams is a commuting square.

Going deeper

Horizontal composition and the interchange law

Exercise 8’s whiskering — pushing a natural transformation through a functor — is half of a richer operation. Given α : F ⇒ G between functors 𝒞 → 𝒟 and β : H ⇒ K between functors 𝒟 → ℰ, there is a horizontal composite β ∗ α : H∘F ⇒ K∘G — a transformation between the composite functors, built from one transformation on each floor. Its component at X can be written two ways: cross with β first and then translate α through K, or translate first and cross second. Exercise 10 shows one naturality square makes the two routes agree.

Vertical composition (§4) and horizontal composition coexist under a compatibility equation — the interchange law, (β′ ∘ β) ∗ (α′ ∘ α) = (β′ ∗ α′) ∘ (β ∗ α) — and the full package (categories, functors, natural transformations, two compositions, interchange) is what “Cat is a 2-category,” the aside of §4, actually means. None of the rest of this course needs the general machinery, but it is worth having seen the doorway: everything in these lessons is the ground floor of a taller building.

§6 — Exercises

Eleven problems on naturality

Naturality proofs are short but the squares must be drawn with care — when in doubt, draw the square first and only then write the equation. The code problems reuse fmap and the Nat checker from §5.

Exercise 1warm-up

Instantiate the naturality square for reverse with the concrete function f = str.upper and the concrete list ['a', 'b'].

Compute both paths — across-then-down and down-then-across — element by element, and confirm they agree. Then say in one sentence why they were bound to agree.

Show solution

Across then down: reverse(['a','b']) = ['b','a'], then map(upper) gives ['B','A']. Down then across: map(upper)(['a','b']) = ['A','B'], then reverse gives ['B','A']. Both paths land on ['B','A'].

They had to: reverse only permutes positions and upper only transforms contents, and the two concerns cannot interact. That separation of concerns is naturality, seen from ground level.

Exercise 2arrows-only

For a functor F, define the family αX = idF X, one identity arrow per object.

Prove this family is a natural transformation F ⇒ F. (It is the identity arrow on F in the functor category — so this exercise checks that the functor category has its identities.)

Show solution

Fix any f : X → Y. The naturality square demands F f ∘ αX = αY ∘ F f, i.e. F f ∘ idF X = idF Y ∘ F f. By the identity law of the target category, both sides are just F f. Every square commutes, so 1F is natural.

Together with Exercise 3 below, this confirms the functor category [𝒞, 𝒟] really is a category in the Lesson 1 sense: identities exist, composites exist, and the laws are inherited pointwise from 𝒟.

Exercise 3arrows-only

Let α : F ⇒ G and β : G ⇒ H be natural transformations, and define the composite componentwise: (β ∘ α)X = βX ∘ αX.

Prove that β ∘ α is natural, and explain how your algebra corresponds to the picture of gluing two naturality squares along their shared middle edge.

Show solution

Fix f : X → Y and compute H f ∘ (βX ∘ αX). Re-associate and apply β’s naturality square (H f ∘ βX = βY ∘ G f) to get βY ∘ (G f ∘ αX); now apply α’s square (G f ∘ αX = αY ∘ F f) to reach (βY ∘ αY) ∘ F f. That is exactly the naturality square for β ∘ α.

Each application of a naturality square is one “pane” of the §4 picture: the first rewrite carries the path across the right square, the second across the left. Two commuting panes glued along the shared column G f make the outer rectangle commute — the algebra and the picture are the same proof.

Exercise 4examples

Decide, with justification, whether each of the following is a natural transformation, and if so between which functors:

  1. double = lambda xs: xs + xs — concatenate a list with itself.
  2. total = lambda xs: sum(xs) — replace a list of numbers by the sum of its elements, proposed as a transformation List ⇒ List.
Show solution

(a) Natural, List ⇒ List. double duplicates and rearranges positions without inspecting elements, so map f (xs + xs) = (map f xs) + (map f xs) for every f — the square commutes.

(b) Not even well-typed as stated. The output of total is a number, not a list, so its components do not have the type List X → List X; and worse, it only exists at numeric element types, whereas a transformation List ⇒ List must supply a component at every type. (A cousin of the idea does work: length : List ⇒ Const ℕ is natural, because length is blind to contents.) The moral: naturality is a property of a family relative to a declared pair of functors — you must say what the source and target are before the question even makes sense.

Exercise 5structure

Let 𝒞 and 𝒟 be preorders viewed as categories, so that functors F, G : 𝒞 → 𝒟 are monotone maps (Lesson 2, §4).

What does a natural transformation F ⇒ G amount to, concretely? How much of the definition survives, and how much becomes automatic?

Show solution

A component αX : F X → G X is an arrow of the preorder 𝒟, which exists precisely when F X ≤ G X — and when it exists it is unique. The naturality squares then commute for free, because preorders are thin (Lesson 1, Exercise 5: every diagram in a thin category commutes).

So a natural transformation between monotone maps is exactly the statement F X ≤ G X for every X — the pointwise order on monotone functions. All the data evaporates; only the condition remains. Thin categories keep doing this: they are the shadow of category theory where every “how” collapses to a “whether.”

Exercise 6code

Take the Nat demo from §5 and sabotage it: change the components from {0:'p', 1:'q'} to {0:'p', 1:'f'}.

Predict what check() will do — which assertion fires, and why — and then run it to confirm.

Show solution

It fails at the component-typing check, before naturality is ever consulted. The component at object 1 must be an arrow F 1 → G 1, i.e. B → D in the square category; but f is typed A → B, so the assertion comparing D.ar[comp[1]] with (F.on_obj[1], G.on_obj[1]) raises immediately.

broken_component.py
bad = Nat(F, G, comp={0:'p', 1:'f'})
try:
    bad.check()
except AssertionError:
    print("rejected: component at 1 is mistyped")

A component with the wrong source and target cannot be rescued by any cleverness elsewhere in the family — typing errors are absolute, which is why the checker tests them first.

Exercise 7examples · new

Consider flatten (often called concat or join), which turns a list of lists into a single list: flatten([[1,2],[3]]) = [1,2,3].

It is a natural transformation — but between which two functors? State the source and target carefully, write down the naturality square, and verify it on the example xss = [[1,2],[3]] with f = lambda x: x*10.

Show solution

The source is the composite functor List ∘ List (lists of lists) and the target is List: flatten : List∘List ⇒ List. The naturality square reads map f (flatten xss) == flatten (map (map f) xss) — note the double map on the right, because the composite functor acts on arrows by map(map f).

On the example: left path, flatten = [1,2,3] then map(*10) = [10,20,30]; right path, map(map(*10)) = [[10,20],[30]] then flatten = [10,20,30]. Equal. flatten is another positions-only operation — it erases inner brackets and touches no elements. (File this one away: flatten is the multiplication of the List monad, and its naturality is one of the monad axioms you’ll meet at the end of the course.)

Exercise 8arrows-only · new

Natural transformations can be pushed forward through a functor. Let α : F ⇒ G be natural, with F, G : 𝒞 → 𝒟, and let H : 𝒟 → ℰ be any functor.

Show that the family with components H(αX) is a natural transformation H∘F ⇒ H∘G. (This operation is called whiskering; it is half of the horizontal composition mentioned in §4’s aside.)

Show solution

Take the naturality square of α at f : X → Y, which is the equation G f ∘ αX = αY ∘ F f, and apply the functor H to both sides. Composition-preservation splits each side: H(G f) ∘ H(αX) = H(αY) ∘ H(F f).

Reading H(G f) as (H∘G)(f) and H(F f) as (H∘F)(f), this is exactly the naturality square for the family H(αX) between the composite functors. The proof is one move — “apply H and use that it preserves composition” — and it is a template: functors transport not just objects, arrows, and diagrams, but entire natural transformations.

Exercise 9code · new

Not every function of type “list to list” is natural — only the ones blind to their elements. Test two candidates numerically:

  1. duplicate = lambda xs: [x for x in xs for _ in (0, 1)], which repeats each element in place: [1,2] ↦ [1,1,2,2].
  2. sorted, Python’s sorting function, proposed as a transformation List ⇒ List.

For each, check the naturality square fmap(f, t(xs)) == t(fmap(f, xs)) on well-chosen inputs. For sorted, find an explicit counterexample and explain what property of sorting is to blame.

Show solution

duplicate is natural — it repeats positions without reading values, so the square commutes for every f and every list.

sorted is not. Take xs = [1, 2] and f = lambda x: -x. One path: sorted([1,2]) = [1,2], then map(f) = [-1,-2]. Other path: map(f) = [-1,-2], then sorted = [-2,-1]. The results differ.

sorted_not_natural.py
dup = lambda xs: [x for x in xs for _ in (0, 1)]
neg = lambda x: -x
assert fmap(neg, dup([1, 2])) == dup(fmap(neg, [1, 2]))   # natural

lhs = fmap(neg, sorted([1, 2]))     # [-1, -2]
rhs = sorted(fmap(neg, [1, 2]))     # [-2, -1]
print(lhs, rhs, lhs == rhs)         # [-1, -2] [-2, -1] False
output[-1, -2] [-2, -1] False

Sorting compares elements — it reads their values to decide the output order — and a non-monotone f like negation changes the verdicts of those comparisons. Blindness to contents is not a stylistic nicety; it is exactly the property the naturality square detects.

Exercise 10arrows-only · new

With α : F ⇒ G (functors 𝒞 → 𝒟) and β : H ⇒ K (functors 𝒟 → ℰ), the two candidate formulas for the horizontal composite’s component at X are:

K(αX) ∘ βF X   and   βG X ∘ H(αX).

Show they are equal. Hint: αX is an arrow of 𝒟 — at which arrow should you instantiate the naturality of β?

Show solution

Instantiate β’s naturality square at the arrow αX : F X → G X of 𝒟. The square reads, verbatim: K(αX) ∘ βF X = βG X ∘ H(αX). That is the required equation — no computation left to do.

The pleasure of this proof is realizing that a component of one transformation is a perfectly ordinary arrow, eligible to be fed into the naturality of another. Layers feeding layers is the everyday texture of the subject from here on: Lesson 7 builds monad multiplication by exactly this kind of move (whiskering a counit), and its associativity proof instantiates a naturality square at a component, just as here.

Exercise 11code · new

Two more candidates for naturality, one of each kind:

  1. take2 = lambda xs: xs[:2] — keep the first two elements (fewer if the list is short).
  2. dedupe = lambda xs: list(dict.fromkeys(xs)) — remove duplicates, keeping first occurrences.

Test each against the naturality square. For the failure, find a counterexample using a non-injective function, and explain what operation on elements the failure exposes. (Note the contrast with Exercise 9: sorted failed via a non-monotone map. Different inspections, different witnesses.)

Show solution

take2 is natural: it selects positions 0 and 1 without reading values, so mapping and truncating commute for every function and list.

dedupe is not — it tests elements for equality, and a non-injective map manufactures new equalities. Counterexample with the constant map:

dedupe_not_natural.py
take2  = lambda xs: xs[:2]
dedupe = lambda xs: list(dict.fromkeys(xs))
const0 = lambda x: 0

for f in (str, lambda x: -x):
    for xs in ([], [1], [3, 1, 2], [5, 5, 1]):
        assert fmap(f, take2(xs)) == take2(fmap(f, xs))   # natural

lhs = fmap(const0, dedupe([1, 2]))    # dedupe sees 1 != 2 ...
rhs = dedupe(fmap(const0, [1, 2]))    # ... but const0 merges them
print(lhs, rhs, lhs == rhs)
output[0, 0] [0] False

On one path the two elements are distinct when dedupe looks, and both survive; on the other they have already been merged, and one is deleted. Equality-testing is content-inspection just as much as comparison is — naturality is a fine-grained instrument, and each failure mode names the exact operation that broke it.

Recap

What to carry forward

  1. A natural transformation α : F ⇒ G is one component arrow αX : F X → G X per object, such that every naturality square G f ∘ αX = αY ∘ F f commutes.
  2. Naturality is uniformity: a family that commutes with the action of every arrow. Parametrically polymorphic functions are natural for free.
  3. A natural isomorphism (every component an iso) is the right notion of two functors being “the same” — the distinction “iso vs. naturally iso” is why the field was founded.
  4. Natural transformations compose by gluing squares, so functors 𝒞 → 𝒟 form the functor category [𝒞, 𝒟]; its isomorphisms are exactly the natural isos.
  5. Slogan to keep: a natural transformation between two arrow-shaped diagrams is a commuting square.
