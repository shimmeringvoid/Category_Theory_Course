Yoneda & Monads

The course’s slogan — an object is known by its arrows — becomes a theorem. And every adjunction, folded in half, becomes a monad you have already used.

§1 — Two punchlines

Cashing the course’s two oldest cheques

Since Lesson 1 this course has repeated one article of faith: never look inside an object; its arrows tell you everything. We have practiced the discipline — arrows-only proofs, universal properties, adjunctions — but the article of faith itself was never proved. The Yoneda lemma proves it. It says, precisely, that the totality of arrows into an object, organized functorially, determines that object up to isomorphism — and even determines the maps between objects. The slogan graduates from methodology to theorem.

The second punchline settles a different account. Lesson 6 built adjunctions F ⊣ U and kept the two categories separate. But nothing stops us composing the two functors into a single endofunctor T = U∘F on 𝒞 — for the free-monoid adjunction, T = List. What structure does T inherit from the adjunction it came from? The answer is a monad: an endofunctor with a unit and a multiplication obeying laws you have met before in another costume. Lesson 3’s Exercise 7 verified that flatten is a natural transformation and filed it away “for the end of the course.” This is the end of the course; flatten is the multiplication of the List monad, and the monad laws are the reason effectful code composes.

§2 — The Yoneda lemma

An object, organized as a functor

Fix an object A of 𝒞. The arrows into A do not form a single set — they form one set per source object, and those sets are related: any arrow g : X → Y converts Y-probes of A into X-probes, by precomposition h ↦ h ∘ g. Sets indexed by objects, translated along arrows — contravariantly, since g points one way and the conversion points the other. That is a functor on the opposite category of Lesson 1, Exercise 7:

Definition · Representable presheaf

A presheaf on 𝒞 is a functor 𝒞op → Set. Each object A gives the representable presheaf yA = 𝒞(−, A): on objects, yA(X) = 𝒞(X, A), the set of arrows X → A; on arrows, precomposition. Functoriality is Lesson 1’s two laws, read backwards: h ∘ id = h and (h ∘ g) ∘ g′ = h ∘ (g ∘ g′).

The presheaf yA is the hero picture made precise: the complete dossier of arrows into A, with its bookkeeping. The Yoneda lemma says the dossier omits nothing. Its general form compares yA with an arbitrary presheaf:

Theorem · The Yoneda lemma

For every presheaf F and every object A, natural transformations yA ⇒ F correspond exactly to elements of F(A):

Nat(yA, F)  ≅  F(A),

via α ↦ αA(idA). A natural transformation out of a representable is determined by where it sends the identity.

Proof. Given α : yA ⇒ F, set a = αA(idA) ∈ F(A). For any f : X → A, the naturality square at f (drawn above) forces αX(f) = F(f)(a) — because f = idA ∘ f, the left edge sends idA to f, and the two paths around the square must agree. So α is completely determined by a: the correspondence is injective. Conversely, any a ∈ F(A) defines a transformation by that same formula αX(f) = F(f)(a), and its naturality squares commute by the functoriality of F (Exercise 2 checks this half in detail). Surjective and injective: a bijection. ∎

“Track the identity” is the entire idea — the identity arrow is the generic probe, and every other probe is the generic one, translated. Two consequences fall out immediately. Taking F = yB: natural transformations yA ⇒ yB correspond to elements of yB(A) = 𝒞(A, B) — the assignment A ↦ yA is a fully faithful functor into the presheaf category (Lesson 3’s functor categories, now indispensable). And fully faithful functors reflect isomorphisms, so:

Corollary · The slogan, q.e.d.

A ≅ B  if and only if  yA ≅ yB. Two objects with the same arrows into them — the same dossier, naturally in the probing object — are isomorphic. An object is the arrows into it. In a poset this says something you can compute: an element is determined by its down-set, and §5 checks it, element by element, in the divisibility order.

§3 — Monads

An adjunction, folded in half

Take any adjunction F ⊣ U and compose: T = U∘F : 𝒞 → 𝒞. The adjunction hands T two natural transformations. The unit η : Id ⇒ T comes straight from Lesson 6. The counit lives in the wrong category — ε : F∘U ⇒ Id on the 𝒟 side — but whiskering it (Lesson 3, Exercise 8, load-bearing at last) with U on the left and F on the right yields μ = UεF : T² ⇒ T, a way to collapse two applications of T into one. For the free-monoid adjunction: T = List, η(x) = [x], and μ evaluates a list of lists by concatenation — μ = flatten.

Definition · Monad

A monad on 𝒞 is an endofunctor T with natural transformations η : Id ⇒ T (unit) and μ : T² ⇒ T (multiplication) such that

  • associativity: μ ∘ μT = μ ∘ Tμ — the two ways to flatten T³ to T agree;
  • unit laws: μ ∘ ηT = id = μ ∘ Tη — wrapping then flattening changes nothing.

Every adjunction produces a monad this way — the laws follow from the triangle identities and naturality, by an argument with exactly the flavor of Lesson 6’s Exercise 4 (this lesson’s Exercise 5 walks it). And the examples are a programmer’s standard library. List: nondeterminism — a computation returning many possible answers; μ merges a batch of batches. Maybe: partiality — T X = X + 1, an answer or None; η wraps, μ collapses Just(Just x) to Just x and everything else to None. Writer: logging — T X = X × M for a monoid M; μ concatenates logs, and its associativity law holds because the monoid’s does — Lesson 1’s axioms, billed at last (Exercise 8 makes this dependency executable). In every case T X reads as “a value of type X, plus an effect,” η as “no effect,” and μ as “merge two rounds of effect into one.”

§4 — The Kleisli category

What the monad laws are for

Here is the question the laws answer. An effectful function from A to B is an ordinary arrow A → T B: it returns a B, plus effect. Can effectful functions be composed? Given f : A → T B and g : B → T C, the types refuse to chain directly — f lands in T B, and g wants a bare B. The monad supplies the adapter: apply T to g (Lesson 2: functors act on arrows), then flatten the doubled effect with μ:

Now the punchline of the whole construction. Define the Kleisli category Kl(T): same objects as 𝒞, but an arrow A ⇸ B is an arrow A → T B of 𝒞, composed as above, with ηA : A → T A as the identity. For this to be a category — Lesson 1’s two laws — you need exactly: composition associative, and η neutral on both sides. Unwind those requirements (Exercise 7) and they are, one for one, the monad laws. The associativity square is what makes chained effectful calls bracket-independent; the unit laws are what make a pure value a no-op in a pipeline. Programmers usually meet the package as bind: m >>= g = μ(T g (m)), Kleisli composition with the first argument already evaluated — Haskell’s do-notation, Rust’s and_then, JavaScript’s promise chaining. The course ends where it began: the two laws of Lesson 1, discovered a second time as the specification of composable effects.

§5 — The finale, machine-checked

Yoneda counted, monad laws by exhaustion

First, the corollary in a poset. Representables in the divisibility order are down-sets — y(a)(x) is inhabited exactly when x | a — and Yoneda says the down-set determines the element, order and all. Sixteen biconditionals, checked:

yoneda_poset.py — an element is its down-set
objs = {1, 2, 3, 6}
downset = {a: {x for x in objs if a % x == 0} for a in objs}

for a in objs:
    for b in objs:
        assert (b % a == 0) == (downset[a] <= downset[b])  # a|b iff ds(a) ⊆ ds(b)

for a in sorted(objs):
    print(f"downset({a}) = {sorted(downset[a])}")
outputdownset(1) = [1] downset(2) = [1, 2] downset(3) = [1, 3] downset(6) = [1, 2, 3, 6]

Second, the lemma itself, counted. In the one-object category Z3, a natural transformation y∗ ⇒ y∗ is a single function α on the three arrows satisfying the naturality condition α(f ∘ g) = α(f) ∘ g. Yoneda predicts exactly |𝒞(∗, ∗)| = 3 of them, each given by postcomposition. Enumerate all 27 candidate functions and see:

yoneda_count.py — Nat(y∗, y∗) has exactly |Hom| elements
from itertools import product as cartesian

H = [0, 1, 2]
comp = lambda a, b: (a + b) % 3            # a . b  in Z3

nats = []
for vals in cartesian(H, repeat=3):
    alpha = dict(zip(H, vals))
    if all(alpha[comp(f, g)] == comp(alpha[f], g)   # naturality
           for f in H for g in H):
        nats.append(alpha)

print(f"natural transformations y* => y* : {len(nats)}")
for a in nats:
    assert all(a[f] == comp(a[0], f) for f in H)    # = postcompose by a[0]
    print(f"  {a}  =  postcompose by {a[0]}")
outputnatural transformations y* => y* : 3 {0: 0, 1: 1, 2: 2} = postcompose by 0 {0: 1, 1: 2, 2: 0} = postcompose by 1 {0: 2, 1: 0, 2: 1} = postcompose by 2

Twenty-seven candidates, three survivors, each one “compose with a fixed arrow” — the bijection Nat(yA, yB) ≅ 𝒞(A, B), witnessed by brute force. Notice where each survivor’s defining arrow shows up: at α(0) = α(id). Track the identity. Finally, the monads. The List monad’s carrier, unit, and multiplication have all appeared in this course before — fmap in Lesson 2, flatten in Lesson 3 — and the laws can be checked over a systematically generated cube of nested lists, with Maybe done fully exhaustively alongside:

monad_laws.py — List and Maybe, laws by enumeration
eta  = lambda x: [x]
mu   = lambda xss: [x for xs in xss for x in xs]     # Lesson 3's flatten
fmap = lambda f, xs: [f(x) for x in xs]

def lists_over(base, maxlen=2):
    out = [[]]
    for k in range(1, maxlen + 1):
        out += [list(t) for t in cartesian(base, repeat=k)]
    return out

T1 = lists_over([0, 1]); T2 = lists_over(T1); T3 = lists_over(T2)

assert all(mu(mu(x)) == mu(fmap(mu, x)) for x in T3)          # associativity
assert all(mu(eta(x)) == x == mu(fmap(eta, x)) for x in T1)   # unit laws
print(f"List : associativity on {len(T3)} T\u00b3 values, units on {len(T1)} — pass")

J     = lambda x: ('Just', x)                        # Maybe's eta
fmapM = lambda f, m: None if m is None else J(f(m[1]))
muM   = lambda mm: None if mm is None else mm[1]     # collapse one layer

M1 = [None] + [J(x) for x in (0, 1)]
M2 = [None] + [J(m) for m in M1]
M3 = [None] + [J(m) for m in M2]

assert all(muM(muM(m)) == muM(fmapM(muM, m)) for m in M3)
assert all(muM(J(m)) == m == muM(fmapM(J, m)) for m in M1)
print(f"Maybe: associativity on {len(M3)} T\u00b3 values, units on {len(M1)} — pass")
outputList : associativity on 3307 T³ values, units on 7 — pass Maybe: associativity on 5 T³ values, units on 3 — pass

For Maybe the check is genuinely exhaustive — there are only five triple-layer values over a two-point base, and all five commute. For List, 3,307 systematically generated triples of nesting all agree that outer-first and inner-first flattening coincide. The downloadable script adds the Writer monad (Exercise 8) and the Kleisli law checks (Exercise 9), completing the set.

Going deeper

Algebras for a monad

A monad describes a kind of structure; an algebra for it is a place where that structure is actually realized. The definition is one arrow and two laws:

Definition · T-algebra

An algebra for a monad (T, η, μ) is an object X with an arrow a : T X → X (the structure map) such that a ∘ ηX = idX and a ∘ μX = a ∘ T a.

Read a as an evaluator: it collapses a T-shaped bundle of X’s to a single X. The first law says a trivially-wrapped value evaluates to itself; the second says a two-layer bundle evaluates to the same answer whether you merge the layers first (μ) or evaluate the inner bundles first (T a). For the List monad, an evaluator that satisfies these laws is exactly a monoid structure — Exercise 10 unwinds the correspondence, closing the loop this course opened when Lesson 1 called monoids one-object categories. The algebras of a monad form the Eilenberg–Moore category, one of the two canonical adjunctions generating the monad — the converse promised in Exercise 5’s solution, and the first stop on the road out of this course.

§6 — Exercises

Eleven problems to finish the course

Exercises 5 and 7 are the capstones — the first connects Lessons 6 and 7, the second connects Lesson 7 back to Lesson 1. Between them they close the course’s longest arcs.

Exercise 1warm-up

In the divisibility order on {1, 2, 3, 6}, write out the four down-sets by hand and verify, for the three pairs (2, 6), (2, 3), and (6, 6), the Yoneda biconditional: a | b exactly when downset(a) ⊆ downset(b).

Then say in one sentence why no two distinct elements of a poset can ever have the same down-set.

Show solution

The down-sets: {1}, {1,2}, {1,3}, {1,2,3,6} — as §5 printed. Pairs: 2 | 6 and {1,2} ⊆ {1,2,3,6} ✓; 2 ∤ 3 and {1,2} ⊄ {1,3} ✓; 6 | 6 and equality of sets ✓.

If downset(a) = downset(b), then since a ∈ downset(a) we get a ∈ downset(b), i.e. a ≤ b, and symmetrically b ≤ a — so a = b by antisymmetry (or a ≅ b in a preorder). Note the proof’s engine: the element belongs to its own down-set because of the identity arrow a ≤ a. Even in this baby case, the identity is the tracked probe — the thin-category shadow of the Yoneda proof.

Exercise 2structure

Complete the surjectivity half of the Yoneda proof. Given a presheaf F and an element a ∈ F(A), define the family αX(f) = F(f)(a) for f : X → A.

Verify the two things §2 left to you: that each naturality square of α commutes (use the functoriality of F — mind the contravariance), and that tracking the identity recovers a: αA(idA) = a.

Show solution

Naturality. Fix g : X → Y. The square requires αX(f ∘ g) = F(g)(αY(f)) for every f : Y → A — precompose first or apply F(g) after. Left side: F(f ∘ g)(a). Right side: F(g)(F(f)(a)). These agree because F, being contravariant, sends f ∘ g to F(g) ∘ F(f) — the flip of Lesson 2’s composition law is exactly what the square needs. (Had F been covariant, the square would demand the un-flipped law; the variance and the direction of translation are made for each other.)

Recovery. αA(idA) = F(idA)(a) = idF(A)(a) = a, by the identity law of F. So “element to transformation to element” is the identity — one of the two round trips of the bijection; §2’s determination argument was the other. Both functor laws of Lesson 2 were consumed, one per round trip, with nothing left over.

Exercise 3arrows-only

Deduce the two headline corollaries from the lemma:

  1. Setting F = yB, show Nat(yA, yB) ≅ 𝒞(A, B), and describe explicitly which transformation an arrow h : A → B becomes.
  2. Conclude that y is fully faithful, and that yA ≅ yB (in the presheaf category) implies A ≅ B (in 𝒞).
Show solution

(a) The lemma with F = yB reads Nat(yA, yB) ≅ yB(A) = 𝒞(A, B). Chasing the bijection: the element h ∈ 𝒞(A, B) becomes the transformation with components αX(f) = yB(f)(h) = h ∘ f — postcomposition by h. That is exactly what §5’s census found: all three survivors were postcompositions, one per arrow.

(b) The bijection is precisely the statement that y is full (surjective on hom-sets) and faithful (injective) — one checks it also respects composition, which is associativity of ∘. A fully faithful functor reflects isomorphisms: if α : yA ≅ yB, write α = y(h) and α−1 = y(k); then y(k ∘ h) = y(k) ∘ y(h) = id = y(idA), and faithfulness cancels the y, giving k ∘ h = idA, likewise h ∘ k = idB. So A ≅ B — the slogan, fully proved, using only machinery this course built.

Exercise 4duality

State the covariant Yoneda lemma — for functors 𝒞 → Set and the co-representable 𝒞(A, −), arrows out of A — by dualizing everything in §2. What does the corollary become, as a slogan?

Show solution

For any covariant F : 𝒞 → Set: Nat(𝒞(A, −), F) ≅ F(A), again via α ↦ αA(idA), with translation now by postcomposition and the determining formula αX(f) = F(f)(a) using F’s covariance. Formally it is §2’s lemma applied to 𝒞op — Lesson 4’s duality principle doing its last day of work in this course.

Slogan: an object is also the arrows out of it. Both dossiers are complete; you may know an object by how everything probes it, or by how it probes everything. (In the poset shadow: an element is determined by its down-set, and equally by its up-set.)

Exercise 5arrows-only

The Lesson 6 connection, in full. Let F ⊣ U with unit η and counit ε, and set T = UF, μ = UεF (components μX = U(εF X)). Prove the three monad laws:

  1. μ ∘ ηT = id — i.e. μX ∘ ηT X = idT X;
  2. μ ∘ Tη = id — i.e. μX ∘ UF(ηX) = idT X;
  3. μ ∘ μT = μ ∘ Tμ.

Hint: (a) and (b) are the two triangle identities, one whiskered by U, the other instantiated at F X. For (c), apply U to the naturality square of ε at the arrow εF X.

Show solution

(a) The second triangle identity says U(εY) ∘ ηU Y = idU Y for every Y; instantiate Y = F X: U(εF X) ∘ ηU F X = id, which is exactly μX ∘ ηT X = idT X.

(b) The first triangle identity says εF X ∘ F(ηX) = idF X; apply the functor U to both sides (Lesson 2: equal arrows have equal images, composition splits): U(εF X) ∘ UF(ηX) = idUF X — which is μX ∘ T(ηX) = idT X.

(c) Naturality of ε at the arrow εF X : FUF X → F X reads εF X ∘ FU(εF X) = εF X ∘ εFUF X. Apply U: μX ∘ UFU(εF X) = μX ∘ U(εFUF X) — and reading the two sides as components, the left is μX ∘ T(μX) and the right is μX ∘ μT X. That is associativity. Three laws, three ingredients: one triangle each for the units, one naturality square for associativity — the same toolkit as Lesson 6’s Exercise 4, run in reverse. (The converse is also true — every monad arises from an adjunction — but its two standard constructions, Kleisli and Eilenberg–Moore, belong to the next course.)

Exercise 6examples

The Maybe monad by cases. With T X = X + 1 represented as None or ('Just', x), and η = Just, μ(None) = None, μ(Just m) = m:

  1. Verify the two unit laws by exhausting the cases of a value m ∈ T X.
  2. Verify associativity by exhausting the cases of a value in T³ X. How many case-shapes are there, and which single line of the definition does all the work?
Show solution

(a) μ(η(m)) = μ(Just(m)) = m — one case, done. For μ(T η(m)): if m = None, Tη maps it to None and μ(None) = None = m; if m = Just(x), Tη(m) = Just(Just(x)) and μ peels a layer: Just(x) = m. ✓

(b) A T³ value has four shapes: None, J(None), J(J(None)), J(J(J(x))) — §5’s exhaustive list, with the last shape once per base element. Chase each through μ ∘ μT and μ ∘ Tμ: e.g. J(J(J(x))) goes to J(J(x)) either way, then to J(x); and any None at any depth propagates to None along both paths. The single line doing all the work is μ(Just m) = m — “peel exactly one layer” — which cannot notice whether the layer it peels is outer or inner. Failure is absorbing, success is transparent: that is the whole personality of Maybe, and the laws are its formal statement.

Exercise 7structure

The full circle. Take the Kleisli data at face value: arrows A ⇸ B are arrows A → T B, composition is g ∘K f = μ ∘ Tg ∘ f, and the proposed identity on A is ηA.

  1. Show f ∘K η = f and η ∘K f = f, identifying which monad unit law each uses (and where naturality of η sneaks in).
  2. Show that associativity of ∘K follows from the monad’s associativity law together with naturality of μ. (Expand both bracketings of h ∘K g ∘K f and slide μ’s past T’s.)
Show solution

(a) Right identity: f ∘K ηA = μ ∘ Tf ∘ ηA. Naturality of η at f : A → TB slides it forward: Tf ∘ ηA = ηT B ∘ f, so the composite is μ ∘ ηT ∘ f = f by the first unit law. Left identity: ηB ∘K f = μ ∘ TηB ∘ f = f directly by the second unit law. Each unit law serves exactly one side — that is why there are two.

(b) Expand: h ∘K (g ∘K f) = μ ∘ Th ∘ μ ∘ Tg ∘ f, while (h ∘K g) ∘K f = μ ∘ T(μ ∘ Th ∘ g) ∘ f = μ ∘ Tμ ∘ T²h ∘ Tg ∘ f. In the first expression, slide Th past μ using μ’s naturality at h: Th ∘ μ = μ ∘ T²h, giving μ ∘ μT ∘ T²h ∘ Tg ∘ f. The two expressions now differ only in their heads — μ ∘ μT versus μ ∘ Tμ — and the associativity square says those are equal. ∎

So: Kl(T) is a category precisely because T is a monad. Lesson 1 asked for two laws and the course spent six lessons discovering how much they carry; here, at the end, the same two laws re-derive themselves as the correctness conditions for effectful programming. The snake has its tail.

Exercise 8code

The Writer monad, and the invoice it sends to Lesson 1. Implement T X = X × M for the string monoid: η(x) = (x, ""), μ(((x, v), w)) = (x, w + v), with fmap acting on the first component.

Verify the three laws on an enumerated grid of values. Then answer: exactly which monoid axiom does each monad law consume? What would break if the “log combiner” were subtraction of numbers instead of concatenation of strings?

Show solution
writer.py
etaW  = lambda x: (x, "")
fmapW = lambda f, p: (f(p[0]), p[1])
def muW(pp):
    (x, inner), outer = pp
    return (x, outer + inner)

logs = ["", "a", "bc"]
W1 = [(x, w) for x in (0, 1) for w in logs]
W2 = [(p, w) for p in W1 for w in logs]
W3 = [(q, w) for q in W2 for w in logs]

assert all(muW(muW(q)) == muW(fmapW(muW, q)) for q in W3)
assert all(muW(etaW(p)) == p == muW(fmapW(etaW, p)) for p in W1)
print(f"Writer: associativity on {len(W3)} values, units on {len(W1)} — pass")
outputWriter: associativity on 54 values, units on 6 — pass

The audit: monad associativity reduces to (w + v) + u = w + (v + u) — the monoid’s associativity; each unit law reduces to "" + w = w or w + "" = w — the monoid’s two-sided unit. A perfect bill: three laws bought, three axioms paid. With subtraction, (w − v) − u ≠ w − (v − u) in general, so associativity fails — replace outer + inner with subtraction on numeric logs and the first assert dies. Lesson 1 defined a monoid as a one-object category; seven lessons later, its two laws turn out to be exactly the price of composable logging.

Exercise 10structure · new

Show that a List-algebra on a set X is the same thing as a monoid structure on X. Given a structure map a : List X → X satisfying the two algebra laws, define e = a([]) and x · y = a([x, y]), and derive:

  1. the unit laws e · x = x = x · e,
  2. associativity of · — evaluate the two-layer value [[x, y], [z]] both ways the algebra law allows.
Show solution

The key consequence of the laws (worth deriving first) is that a evaluates concatenations pairwise: applying the second law to [xs, ys] ∈ List(List X) gives a(xs ⧺ ys) = a([a(xs), a(ys)])= a(xs) · a(ys).

(a) e · x = a([a([]), a([x])]); by the pairwise rule backwards this is a([] ⧺ [x]) = a([x]), and the first algebra law (a ∘ η = id) gives a([x]) = x. The right unit law mirrors.

(b) Evaluate [[x, y], [z]]: merging layers first (μ) gives a([x, y, z]); evaluating inner bundles first (T a) gives a([x·y, z]) = (x·y)·z. The algebra law equates them, so a([x,y,z]) = (x·y)·z — and running the same computation on [[x], [y, z]] gives a([x,y,z]) = x·(y·z). Both bracketings equal the same three-element evaluation: associativity. Conversely a monoid defines a = “multiply the list out” (Lesson 6’s fold_map of the identity), and the two constructions invert each other. Monoids are the algebras of the List monad — the course’s first definition and its last, revealed as the same idea seen from opposite ends.

Recap

What the course leaves you with

  1. The Yoneda lemma: Nat(yA, F) ≅ F(A), proved by tracking the identity. Its corollary is the course’s creed as a theorem — A ≅ B iff yA ≅ yB: an object is the arrows into it.
  2. The Yoneda embedding is fully faithful: maps between objects are exactly natural transformations between their dossiers — in a poset, elements are their down-sets.
  3. A monad is an endofunctor with unit and multiplication obeying associativity and unit laws; every adjunction yields one via T = UF, μ = UεF. List, Maybe, and Writer are the standard library: nondeterminism, partiality, logging.
  4. The Kleisli category of effectful arrows A → T B is a category precisely because the monad laws hold — Lesson 1’s two axioms, rediscovered as the specification of composable effects. bind is Kleisli composition.
  5. Everything above is checkable by machine in finite settings: Yoneda counted (27 candidates, 3 survivors, all postcompositions), monad laws by enumeration (3,307 List instances; Maybe exhaustively). The subject’s abstractions cash out as assertions that pass.
