Intro to Category Theory · Lesson 05

Limits & Colimits

Terminal objects, products, and their cousins were the same construction wearing different diagrams. Time to say so — and prove the uniqueness theorem exactly once.

§1 — One pattern, many disguises

Every construction so far was a cone in denial

Look back at Lesson 4 with suspicious eyes. A terminal object: an object such that everything maps to it, uniquely. A product: an object with arrows to A and B, such that anything else with arrows to A and B factors through it, uniquely. Both times the recipe was the same: fix some target configuration, consider objects equipped with arrows to that configuration, and crown the best one. The only thing that changed was the shape of the configuration — nothing at all, or two isolated dots.

We have had the vocabulary for “shape” since Lesson 2. A diagram in 𝒞 of shape 𝒥 is just a functor D : 𝒥 → 𝒞, where 𝒥 is a small index category: its objects mark dots and its arrows mark which connecting arrows the diagram must supply. Lesson 2’s Exercise 6 already proved the first instance — functors out of 𝟚 are exactly arrows — and Lesson 3’s checker demo showed that a natural transformation between two such diagrams is a commuting square. This lesson is where that machinery stops being an aside and becomes the main act.

The plan: define a cone over a diagram (§2), define the limit as the best cone, and then get the uniqueness theorem for every limit at once by a one-line reduction to Lesson 4. Then we tour the shapes (§3): the two we know, plus two new ones — equalizers and pullbacks — that turn out to be solution sets and database joins. Duality flips everything into colimits (§4), and in §5 a single Python function computes all of it.

§2 — Cones & limits

The best vantage point over a diagram

Definition · Cone

Let D : 𝒥 → 𝒞 be a diagram. A cone over D is an object X (the apex) together with one arrow xj : X → D(j) for each object j of 𝒥 (the legs), such that every triangle commutes: for every arrow a : i → j in 𝒥,

D(a) ∘ xi = xj.

That is the hero picture: an apex, a leg to every object of the diagram, and no way to cheat — going directly to D(j) must agree with going via any D(i). Lesson 2’s Exercise 8 planted a flag we can now collect: a cone with apex X is precisely a natural transformation ΔX ⇒ D from the constant functor at X. The legs are the components, and the cone condition is naturality — check it against the square, remembering that ΔX sends every arrow to idX.

Cones over a fixed D form a category of their own, Cone(D): an arrow from cone (X, x) to cone (L, λ) is an arrow m : X → L of 𝒞 that is compatible with all the legs, λj ∘ m = xj for every j. (Identities and composites are inherited from 𝒞; checking the axioms is Exercise 3.) With that category in hand, the central definition of the lesson costs four words:

Definition · Limit

A limit of D is a terminal object of Cone(D).

Unwound: a cone (lim D, λ) such that every cone (X, x) factors through it via exactly one arrow m : X → lim D with λj ∘ m = xj for all j.

Theorem · Uniqueness, once and for all

Any two limits of the same diagram are isomorphic via a unique cone-compatible isomorphism.

Proof. A limit is a terminal object in Cone(D), and Lesson 4 §2 proved that terminal objects are unique up to unique isomorphism — in any category. Apply that theorem to Cone(D). ∎

This is the promised payoff. In Lesson 4 we proved uniqueness for terminal objects, then re-proved it by hand for products (Exercise 5) and noted it dualizes. Now every limit of every shape — including every construction in the rest of this lesson — inherits the theorem from one four-sentence proof, applied in a cleverly chosen category. Building the right category and citing an old theorem inside it is one of the field’s signature moves; this is its first full appearance.

§3 — Shapes and their limits

Rows one and two are Lesson 4 in new clothes. Over the empty diagram, a cone is just an apex — no legs, no conditions — so the terminal cone is the terminal object. Over two isolated dots picking out A and B, a cone is an object with arrows to each, the triangles are vacuous, and the terminal cone is exactly the product with its projections. (Exercise 1 has you verify both unwindings carefully.) The new rows are where the shape starts doing real work.

The parallel pair: equalizers

Let 𝒥 be two objects with two parallel arrows between them, and let the diagram pick out f, g : A → B. A cone is an apex X with legs to A and B — but the triangle conditions force the B-leg to equal both f ∘ xA and g ∘ xA. So a cone is really just an arrow x : X → A with f ∘ x = g ∘ x — an arrow that cannot tell f and g apart. The limit, written e : E → A and called the equalizer, is the universal such arrow.

In Set the equalizer is the solution set: everything in A where the equation f(a) = g(a) holds, sitting inside A via the inclusion map. Solving equations — the oldest activity in mathematics — is computing a limit over the second-smallest interesting shape. And the universal property captures precisely what a solution set is for: any way of landing in A that satisfies the equation lands, in exactly one way, in the solutions.

The cospan: pullbacks

Now let 𝒥 be the cospan • → • ← •, so the diagram is a pair of arrows with a common target: f : A → Z and g : B → Z. A cone is an apex with arrows to A and B that agree upon arrival in Z (the leg to Z is forced, just as with the equalizer). The limit is the pullback A ×Z B — the product of A and B, constrained by the shared map to Z.

Pullbacks are everywhere once you know the shape. Two subsets included in a common set pull back to their intersection. A function f : A → B pulled back along a subset inclusion S ↪ B gives the inverse image f−1(S) (Exercise 5). And two database tables sharing a key column pull back to their join: the pairs of rows that agree on the key — which is exactly what §5 computes. When the maps to Z carry the meaning “this row’s foreign key,” the pullback carries the meaning “match them up.”

§4 — Colimits

Reverse the legs, glue instead of carve

Duality now does a lesson’s worth of work in a paragraph. A cocone under a diagram D is an apex X with legs D(j) → X out of the diagram, commuting with the diagram’s arrows; equivalently, a natural transformation D ⇒ ΔX. A colimit is an initial cocone — the universal way to fuse the diagram into a single object — and it is unique up to unique isomorphism because Lesson 4’s theorem dualizes (or directly: apply the terminal-object theorem in Cocone(D)op).

The zoo dualizes row by row. The colimit of the empty diagram is the initial object; of two dots, the coproduct. The dual of the equalizer is the coequalizer of f, g : A → B: in Set, the quotient of B by the equivalence relation generated by f(a) ∼ g(a) — where equalizers solve equations, coequalizers impose them, which is how quotient constructions throughout mathematics arise. The dual of the pullback is the pushout: the universal way to glue A and B along a shared part Z, as when two line segments are joined at an endpoint, or two sets are united over their intersection (Exercise 7). Limits carve out; colimits glue together.

Aside · Two shapes suffice

A category with all binary products, a terminal object, and all equalizers automatically has every finite limit — pullbacks, and limits over any finite shape whatsoever, can be assembled from those parts. Exercise 8 walks through the first case: a pullback is an equalizer of two maps out of a product. This is why “Set has all (small) limits” is provable by exhibiting just two constructions, and it is the theorem quietly powering §5: our finder searches for arbitrary limits, but a library implementer would only ever need to hand-build two.

§5 — Limits in Python

One finder to rule the whole zoo

Lesson 4’s code had one finder per construction — terminal_objects, find_products, find_coproducts. This lesson’s definitions say those should all be a single function, parameterized by a diagram. A diagram is a Functor from Lesson 2; a cone is an apex plus a legs dictionary; and the limit is found by checking the terminal-cone property by enumeration. The whole finder is shorter than any two of Lesson 4’s specialists combined:

limits.py — the general limit finder
def cones(C, D):
    "All cones over the diagram D : J -> C, as (apex, legs) pairs."
    J, out = D.C, []
    Jobjs = sorted(J.obj, key=str)
    for X in C.obj:
        legsets = [arrows_between(C, X, D.on_obj[j]) for j in Jobjs]
        for combo in cartesian(*legsets):
            legs = dict(zip(Jobjs, combo))
            # every triangle commutes:  D(a) . leg_i == leg_j
            if all(C.comp[(D.on_arr[a], legs[i])] == legs[j]
                   for a, (i, j) in J.ar.items()):
                out.append((X, legs))
    return out

def find_limits(C, D):
    "Limit = the cone through which every cone factors exactly once."
    cs = cones(C, D)
    return [(apex, legs) for apex, legs in cs
            if all(len([m for m in arrows_between(C, X, apex)
                        if all(C.comp[(legs[j], m)] == l[j]
                               for j in legs)]) == 1
                   for X, l in cs)]

Read the receipts: the empty diagram yields the terminal object 6; two dots yield the product gcd(2,3) = 1 with the same projections find_products found last lesson; and the cospan yields the pullback — which in a thin category is again the meet, its extra leg forced. The colimit of two dots is the coproduct lcm(2,3) = 6, via find_colimits, the arrow-reversed twin included in the downloadable script. Four Lesson-4 functions have become one function and a shape parameter. Finally, the two new constructions in Set itself, where they earn their keep:

set_limits.py — equalizers solve, pullbacks join
# equalizer of f, g : X -> Y  ==  the solution set of  f(x) = g(x)
X = range(-3, 4)
f = lambda x: x * x
g = lambda x: x + 2
E = [x for x in X if f(x) == g(x)]
print("equalizer of x\u00b2 and x+2 on [-3..3]:", E)

# pullback of  dept : Emp -> Key  and  key : Bldg -> Key  ==  a join
employees = [("Alice", "eng"), ("Bob", "sales"), ("Carol", "eng")]
buildings = [("eng", "Building 1"), ("sales", "Building 2")]
dept = lambda e: e[1]
key  = lambda b: b[0]

P  = [(e, b) for e in employees for b in buildings if dept(e) == key(b)]
p1 = lambda x: x[0]
p2 = lambda x: x[1]

for e, b in P:
    print(f"{e[0]} works in {b[1]}")
print("square commutes:", all(dept(p1(x)) == key(p2(x)) for x in P))

The equalizer of x² and x + 2 is {−1, 2} — the roots of x² − x − 2, computed as a limit. And the pullback of two tables over their shared key column is the inner join, projections and commuting square included. SELECT ... JOIN ... ON has been a limit over a cospan all along.

Going deeper

The pasting lemma

Pullbacks compose in a way worth knowing by name. Draw two commuting squares side by side, sharing a vertical edge — a wide rectangle subdivided into a left square and a right square. The pasting lemma says: if the right square is a pullback, then the outer rectangle is a pullback if and only if the left square is.

This is the working tool behind many “obvious” manipulations: computing an iterated inverse image f−1(g−1(S)) in one step, or chaining two joins on shared keys and knowing the result is itself a join. Categorically it says pullbacks paste like tiles — the same gluing behavior that naturality squares showed in Lesson 3, Exercise 3, now upgraded from “commuting” to “universal.” Exercise 10 proves the harder direction; the proof is three universal properties taking turns, in the style this course has been rehearsing since Lesson 4.

§6 — Exercises

Eleven problems on limits and colimits

The unwinding exercises (1, 2, 5) are where the lesson sinks in: take the general definition, specialize the shape, and watch a familiar object appear. The arrows-only proofs then use the universal property the way Lesson 4 taught — existence to manufacture arrows, uniqueness to collapse them.

Exercise 1warm-up

Carefully unwind the definition of a limit over the two smallest shapes:

  1. The empty index category. What is a cone over the empty diagram? What does the terminal-cone condition say? Conclude that the limit is exactly a terminal object of 𝒞.
  2. The discrete two-object index category (two dots, identity arrows only). Show that a cone is exactly “an object with an arrow to A and an arrow to B,” and that the limit condition reproduces, word for word, Lesson 4’s definition of the product.
Show solution

(a) With no objects in 𝒥 there are no legs to give and no triangles to check, so a cone is just an apex: every object of 𝒞 is one, in exactly one way. A cone morphism (X) → (L) is any arrow X → L — the compatibility conditions are vacuous too. So Cone(D) is just 𝒞 itself, and its terminal object is a terminal object of 𝒞.

(b) The only arrows of 𝒥 are identities, and the triangle for idj reads D(idj) ∘ xj = xj — automatic, since functors preserve identities. So a cone is an unconstrained pair of arrows xA : X → A, xB : X → B. The terminal cone (P, π1, π2) must admit, from every such pair, exactly one m with π1 ∘ m = xA and π2 ∘ m = xB — which is Lesson 4’s product definition verbatim, with ⟨xA, xB⟩ = m.

Exercise 2warm-up

What is the limit of a diagram over the one-object, one-arrow index category 𝟙 — i.e. of a diagram that just picks out a single object A of 𝒞?

Guess first, then verify your guess against the universal property.

Show solution

The limit is A itself, with the identity as its single leg. A cone is any arrow x : X → A (the one triangle, over id, is automatic). The cone (A, idA) is terminal among these: given x : X → A, a mediating arrow m must satisfy idA ∘ m = x, forcing m = x — existence and uniqueness in one line.

So “the limit of a single object is that object”: reassuringly boring, and a useful sanity check that the machinery adds nothing when there is nothing to add. (Dually, the colimit over 𝟙 is also A.)

Exercise 3structure

§2 asserted that cones over a fixed diagram D form a category Cone(D). Verify the claim: say what the identity on a cone (X, x) is, show that the composite of two cone morphisms is a cone morphism, and explain where the category laws come from.

Show solution

The identity on (X, x) is idX, which is leg-compatible since xj ∘ idX = xj. For composites, suppose m : (X, x) → (Y, y) and n : (Y, y) → (L, λ) are cone morphisms, so yj ∘ m = xj and λj ∘ n = yj for all j. Then λj ∘ (n ∘ m) = (λj ∘ n) ∘ m = yj ∘ m = xj — compatible again, using associativity once per step.

Associativity and the identity laws hold because composition in Cone(D) is composition in 𝒞, merely restricted to arrows that happen to respect legs. Categories whose arrows are “arrows of 𝒞 satisfying extra conditions” inherit the laws for free — the same phenomenon as the injective-functions category of Lesson 1, Exercise 4.

Exercise 4examples

Compute two equalizers in Set, concretely:

  1. The equalizer of f(x) = x³ and g(x) = x on the set X = {−2, −1, 0, 1, 2}.
  2. The equalizer of any f : A → B with itself. What does the answer say, in slogan form, about what equalizers measure?
Show solution

(a) Solve x³ = x, i.e. x(x−1)(x+1) = 0: the equalizer is {−1, 0, 1}, included into X. (One comprehension in Python: [x for x in X if x**3 == x].)

(b) The condition f(a) = f(a) holds everywhere, so the equalizer of f with itself is all of A, with e = idA (up to the usual unique isomorphism). Slogan: an equalizer measures disagreement — it is the part of the domain on which two arrows cannot be told apart, and two copies of the same arrow disagree nowhere. This also explains why equalizers are invisible in thin categories: parallel arrows are already equal there, so every equalizer is trivial — and why §5 demonstrated equalizers in Set rather than in the divisibility poset.

Exercise 5structure

Inverse images are pullbacks. Let f : A → B be a function and S ⊆ B a subset, with inclusion map i : S ↪ B.

Compute the pullback A ×B S of f and i as a set of pairs, and exhibit a bijection between it and the inverse image f−1(S) = { a ∈ A : f(a) ∈ S }. Try it concretely with A = {1,2,3,4}, f(a) = a mod 2, B = {0,1}, S = {0}.

Show solution

The pullback is { (a, s) : f(a) = i(s) } = { (a, s) : f(a) = s, s ∈ S }. Since the second coordinate is forced to equal f(a), the map (a, s) ↦ a is a bijection onto { a : f(a) ∈ S } = f−1(S), with inverse a ↦ (a, f(a)). Concretely: the pairs are {(2, 0), (4, 0)}, and the bijection lands on f−1({0}) = {2, 4} — the even elements.

So “take the preimage” is “pull back along an inclusion.” This is where the name comes from: the subset S of the codomain is pulled back along f to a subset of the domain. Filtering a dataset by a condition on a derived column is this construction, performed daily.

Exercise 6arrows-only

An arrow m : X → Y is a monomorphism (is monic) if it is cancellable on the left: whenever m ∘ h = m ∘ k for arrows h, k : W → X, it follows that h = k. (In Set, monic = injective.)

Prove that every equalizer arrow is monic: if e : E → A is the equalizer of f, g : A → B, and e ∘ h = e ∘ k, then h = k. Hint: name the common composite x = e ∘ h, check that x is a cone, and let uniqueness of mediators finish.

Show solution

Let x = e ∘ h = e ∘ k : W → A. It equalizes the pair: f ∘ x = (f ∘ e) ∘ h = (g ∘ e) ∘ h = g ∘ x, using f ∘ e = g ∘ e (the equalizer’s own fork) and associativity. So x is a cone over the parallel pair, and the universal property says there is exactly one arrow m : W → E with e ∘ m = x.

But two such arrows are staring at us: h and k both satisfy e ∘ (–) = x — that is how x was defined. Uniqueness forces h = k. ∎ This is the cleanest specimen yet of the Lesson-4 proof pattern: to show two arrows are equal, exhibit both as mediators for the same cone and let “exactly one” do the work. In Set it recovers the obvious fact that a subset inclusion is injective — but the proof never looked at an element, so it holds in every category with equalizers.

Exercise 7duality

Dualize §3’s pullback discussion:

  1. State the definition of the pushout of a span A ← Z → B by reversing every arrow in the pullback’s universal property.
  2. In Set, let Z = A ∩ B with the two inclusions into A = {1, 2} and B = {2, 3}. Compute the pushout and identify it with a familiar set. What happened to the two copies of the element 2?
Show solution

(a) Given f : Z → A and g : Z → B, the pushout is an object A +Z B with arrows j1 : A → A +Z B and j2 : B → A +Z B satisfying j1 ∘ f = j2 ∘ g, such that for any X with p : A → X, q : B → X and p ∘ f = q ∘ g, there is exactly one m : A +Z B → X with m ∘ j1 = p and m ∘ j2 = q.

(b) Start with the coproduct — the disjoint union with tags, {('L',1), ('L',2), ('R',2), ('R',3)} from Lesson 4 — and then impose the equation j1(f(z)) = j2(g(z)) for each z ∈ Z = {2}: glue ('L',2) to ('R',2). The result has three points and is in bijection with the plain union {1, 2, 3}. Where Lesson 4’s coproduct kept the two 2’s apart, the pushout is precisely the instruction to merge them — union is coproduct relative to the intersection. (And the recipe “coproduct, then coequalize” is the dual of the aside in §4: pushouts from coproducts and coequalizers.)

Exercise 8think

Prove the first case of §4’s amber aside: a category with binary products and equalizers has all pullbacks.

Given f : A → Z and g : B → Z, form the product A × B and the two composites f ∘ π1, g ∘ π2 : A × B → Z. Show that their equalizer e : E → A × B, equipped with the legs π1 ∘ e and π2 ∘ e, satisfies the pullback’s universal property. (Sanity-check the construction in Set first: what set does it build?)

Show solution

In Set the recipe builds { (a,b) ∈ A × B : f(a) = g(b) } — exactly the pullback of §3 — which is the right intuition to carry into the general proof.

The square commutes: f ∘ (π1 ∘ e) = (f ∘ π1) ∘ e = (g ∘ π2) ∘ e = g ∘ (π2 ∘ e), the middle step being the equalizer’s fork. Universality: take any X with p : X → A, q : X → B, f ∘ p = g ∘ q. The product gives ⟨p, q⟩ : X → A × B, and it equalizes the pair — (f ∘ π1) ∘ ⟨p,q⟩ = f ∘ p = g ∘ q = (g ∘ π2) ∘ ⟨p,q⟩ — so it factors through e via a unique m : X → E, and the legs check out: (π1 ∘ e) ∘ m = π1 ∘ ⟨p,q⟩ = p, likewise for q.

Uniqueness of m: any competitor m′ with the same leg equations has e ∘ m′ = ⟨p, q⟩ — because e ∘ m′ hits p and q under the projections, and the product’s uniqueness clause pins such an arrow down — and then Exercise 6 (equalizer arrows are monic) forces m′ = m. Three universal properties, each contributing its uniqueness exactly where needed: this is what “assembling limits from parts” looks like in practice.

Exercise 9code

Extend §5’s zoo with a shape it has not seen: the span • ← • → •. Build the index category (three objects; two non-identity arrows out of the middle object), define the diagram in Div sending it to 2 ← 1 → 3, and compute both find_limits and find_colimits of it.

Exercise 10think · new

Set up the pasting lemma’s notation: objects X, Y, Z across the top, A, B, C across the bottom, vertical arrows x, y, z downward, horizontal arrows completing a left square (X, Y, A, B) and a right square (Y, Z, B, C), both commuting. Assume the right square is a pullback.

Prove one direction: if the left square is a pullback, the outer rectangle is. (Take a cone over the outer cospan — T → A and T → Z agreeing in C — and factor it in two stages, right square first.)

Show solution

Let a : T → A and t : T → Z agree in C. Stage one: composing a across the bottom gives T → B, which together with t forms a cone over the right cospan (they agree in C by assumption); the right pullback yields a unique u : T → Y compatible with both. Stage two: now a and u form a cone over the left cospan (compatibility from stage one is exactly agreement in B); the left pullback yields a unique v : T → X, and chasing the two triangles shows v is compatible with the outer rectangle’s legs a and t.

Uniqueness: a competitor v′ for the outer rectangle composes to a T → Y that satisfies stage one’s conditions, so it equals u by the right square’s uniqueness; then v′ satisfies stage two’s conditions and equals v by the left square’s. Existence built in two stages, uniqueness collapsed in two stages — Lesson 4’s division of labor, run twice in sequence. (The converse direction, outer-implies-left, is the same game with the roles of the cones rearranged.)

Exercise 11code · new

§5 demonstrated equalizers in Set because thin categories have none worth seeing. Fix that: build the smallest category with a genuine equalizer — the fork. Objects E, A, B; non-identity arrows e : E → A, a parallel pair f, g : A → B, and their common composite w = f ∘ e = g ∘ e.

Prediction: the equalizer should be (E, e) — the only cone over the pair, since f ∘ idA ≠ g ∘ idA rules out apex A — with mediator uniqueness from E’s only endomorphism being idE.

fork.py
fk_ar = {'iE':('E','E'),'iA':('A','A'),'iB':('B','B'),
         'e':('E','A'),'f':('A','B'),'g':('A','B'),'w':('E','B')}
ident = {'E':'iE','A':'iA','B':'iB'}
fk_comp = {}
for x, (u, v) in fk_ar.items():
    fk_comp[(ident[v], x)] = x
    fk_comp[(x, ident[u])] = x
fk_comp[('f','e')] = 'w'
fk_comp[('g','e')] = 'w'          # both composites land on w
Fork = FiniteCategory({'E','A','B'}, fk_ar, fk_comp, ident)

pp_ar = {'i0':(0,0),'i1':(1,1),'u':(0,1),'v':(0,1)}   # parallel pair
pp_comp = {('i0','i0'):'i0',('i1','i1'):'i1',
           ('u','i0'):'u',('i1','u'):'u',
           ('v','i0'):'v',('i1','v'):'v'}
Pair = FiniteCategory({0,1}, pp_ar, pp_comp, {0:'i0',1:'i1'})

D_fg = Functor(Pair, Fork, {0:'A',1:'B'},
               {'i0':'iA','i1':'iB','u':'f','v':'g'})
print(find_limits(Fork, D_fg))
output[('E', {0: 'e', 1: 'w'})]

Confirmed: the finder returns exactly the fork’s handle, legs e and the forced w. This is the course’s first machine-computed limit in a category that is not thin — the parallel arrows f ≠ g are what make the equalizer’s job nontrivial, and seven arrows are all it takes.

Recap

What to carry forward

  1. A diagram is a functor D : 𝒥 → 𝒞 from an index category; a cone is an apex with commuting legs to it — equivalently, a natural transformation ΔX ⇒ D. The last three lessons’ machinery, cashed in.
  2. A limit is a terminal object in the category of cones — so one application of Lesson 4’s theorem gives uniqueness up to unique isomorphism for every limit of every shape, forever.
  3. The shape is a dial: empty ↦ terminal object, two dots ↦ product, parallel pair ↦ equalizer (solution sets), cospan ↦ pullback (intersections, inverse images, joins).
  4. Colimits are the duals — initial cocones: coproducts, coequalizers (quotients), pushouts (gluing). Limits carve out; colimits glue together.
  5. Products plus equalizers generate all finite limits — and in a finite category the whole theory is one search function with the shape as a parameter.
