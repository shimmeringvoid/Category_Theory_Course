Intro to Category Theory · Lesson 06

Adjunctions

Two functors pointing opposite ways, and a perfect exchange of arrows between them. The concept category theory was invented to state.

§1 — What does “free” mean?

A word we have owed a definition since Lesson 2

Lesson 2 introduced the forgetful functor U : Mon → Set and gestured at its partner going the other way: the free monoid functor. It is time to pay that debt. The free monoid on a set X is the set of finite lists over X, with concatenation as the operation and the empty list as the unit — but “lists with concatenation” is an implementation. What is the interface? What property makes this monoid, and no other, the free one?

Here it is. To give a monoid homomorphism out of List X into any monoid M, it is enough — and necessary — to say where the generators go: any function f : X → U M extends to exactly one homomorphism f̂ : List X → M, namely f̂([x₁, …, xₙ]) = f(x₁) · ⋯ · f(xₙ). Programmers know this operation as foldMap. “Free” means: no relations beyond the forced ones, so homomorphisms out are as easy as functions in.

Squint at the phrase “exactly one” and you will recognize a universal property; but this one has a new shape. It is not about arrows into a single object — it is a correspondence between two different kinds of arrow, living in two different categories:

Mon(List X, M)  ≅  Set(X, U M).

Homomorphisms from the free thing on one side; plain functions to the underlying set on the other; a perfect bijection between them. Abstract the pattern — replace List and U by any pair of opposite-pointing functors — and you have the definition of an adjunction. This lesson is a tour of how much of mathematics turns out to have exactly this shape.

§2 — The definition

Arrows traded at par

Definition · Adjunction

Let F : 𝒞 → 𝒟 and U : 𝒟 → 𝒞 be functors. An adjunction F ⊣ U (“F is left adjoint to U”) is a bijection

𝒟(F X, Y)  ≅  𝒞(X, U Y)

for every X in 𝒞 and Y in 𝒟, natural in both. The matched arrows f̂ : F X → Y and f : X → U Y are called each other’s transposes.

The mnemonic for the direction: the left adjoint appears on the left of the hom-set. And “natural in both” is not fine print — it is what makes the bijection a single coherent conversion rather than a family of coincidences. Concretely it says transposition commutes with composition on both flanks: for any h : X′ → X in 𝒞 and k : Y → Y′ in 𝒟, the transpose of k ∘ f̂ ∘ F h is U k ∘ f ∘ h. Pre-process the input or post-process the output, then transpose — or transpose first and do the same on the other side; you land on the same arrow. (This is the naturality of Lesson 3, applied in both variables at once; the checker-friendly square formulation appears in Exercise 4.)

In the free-monoid case the bijection is exactly foldMap one way and “restrict to generators” the other. And the definition immediately explains a Lesson 2 mystery: why the forgetful functor felt canonical while ordinary functions felt structureless. They are two halves of one mechanism — U throws away structure, F adds the least possible structure, and the adjunction is the precise sense in which those are inverse tendencies without being inverse functors.

Currying

Fix a set A. A function of two arguments X × A → Y and a function-valued function X → YA carry the same information — curry and uncurry convert between them, and the conversion respects pre- and post-composition. That is an adjunction (−) × A ⊣ (−)A, and it is the universal property that defines function types: YA is the object that makes the bijection Set(X × A, Y) ≅ Set(X, YA) work. Galois connections: adjunctions between posets

Run the definition in thin categories. Functors between posets are monotone maps (Lesson 2), each hom-set has at most one element, so “bijection of hom-sets” collapses to a two-way implication: monotone f and g form an adjunction f ⊣ g exactly when

f(x) ≤ y  ⟺  x ≤ g(y)   for all x, y.

Order theorists call this a Galois connection. The everyday example: include the integers into the reals, i : ℤ ↪ ℝ. Then ⌈r⌉ ≤ n ⟺ r ≤ n makes ceiling a left adjoint of the inclusion, and n ≤ ⌊r⌋ ⟺ n ≤ r makes floor a right adjoint: ⌈−⌉ ⊣ i ⊣ ⌊−⌋. Rounding up is the best over-approximation, rounding down the best under-approximation — left adjoints approach from one side, right adjoints from the other, and the inclusion sits between its own two corrections. §5 verifies both equivalences on 1,586 instances, and builds a finder that computes adjoints of arbitrary monotone maps between finite posets.

The diagonal, flanked

Lesson 4’s constructions join the gallery too. The diagonal functor Δ : 𝒞 → 𝒞 × 𝒞 sends X to the pair (X, X); a right adjoint to it must convert arrows (X, X) → (A, B) — i.e. pairs of arrows — into single arrows X → ?, and Lesson 4’s product definition says the product does exactly that. Dually the coproduct is Δ’s left adjoint: + ⊣ Δ ⊣ ×, another sandwich (Exercise 6). This generalizes: replacing Δ by the constant-diagram functor of Lesson 5, limits are right adjoints and colimits are left adjoints — the previous two lessons were secretly case studies of this one.

§4 — Unit, counit, and consequences

The adjunction, repackaged as two natural transformations

The bijection can be distilled into two canonical arrows. Transpose the identity idF X : F X → F X and get the unit ηX : X → U F X; transpose idU Y the other way and get the counit εY : F U Y → Y. Naturality of the bijection makes η : Id ⇒ U∘F and ε : F∘U ⇒ Id natural transformations — Lesson 3’s machinery, in a starring role. In the free-monoid adjunction, η is the singleton map x ↦ [x] (“a generator, regarded as a one-term word”) and ε evaluates a formal word of monoid elements down to an actual product. In currying, ε is eval.

Definition · Adjunction, take two

An adjunction F ⊣ U may equivalently be given by natural transformations η : Id ⇒ U∘F and ε : F∘U ⇒ Id satisfying the two triangle identities:

εF X ∘ F(ηX) = idF X   and   U(εY) ∘ ηU Y = idU Y.

Each direction of the equivalence is a construction: from a bijection, transpose identities to get η, ε and derive the triangles; from η, ε, define transposition by f̂ ↦ U f̂ ∘ ηX and f ↦ εY ∘ F f, and the triangle identities are exactly what force these to be mutually inverse. Exercise 4 walks the second direction in full — it is the best composition workout the course has to offer. In the free-monoid case the triangles say something you can whisper in plain language: “wrap each generator as a word, then evaluate: you get the word back” and “wrap a monoid element, then evaluate: you get the element back.”

Theorem · RAPL

Right adjoints preserve limits; left adjoints preserve colimits. If U has a left adjoint, then U(lim D) ≅ lim (U∘D) for every diagram D with a limit — and dually for F and colimits.

You have known an instance of this for years: the underlying set of a product of groups is the product of the underlying sets. Now you know why — U : Grp → Set has a left adjoint (free groups), so it must preserve products, terminal objects, equalizers, pullbacks, all of Lesson 5’s zoo at once. The contrapositive is a weapon: a functor that fails to preserve even one colimit cannot have a right adjoint, full stop. Exercise 7 uses it to prove a non-existence theorem in two lines, and §5’s finder shows the same phenomenon numerically: the monotone map that crushes the divisibility poset’s bottom element fails to preserve the initial object, and the search for its right adjoint comes back empty.

Aside · Adjoints are unique

If U has a left adjoint, it has exactly one up to natural isomorphism — the adjunction pins its partner down, just as universal properties pinned objects down in Lesson 4 (and for the same reason; Exercise 5 makes the connection concrete for free monoids). This is why one says the free monoid, the exponential, the product. §5’s finder returns a list of all right adjoints of a given map; in every successful run, that list has length exactly one.

§5 — Adjunctions in Python

Bijections you can run, adjoints you can search for

Three experiments, in increasing order of generality. First, the floor/ceiling sandwich: a Galois connection is a finite conjunction of biconditionals, so it can be checked wholesale on a grid of test values — both adjunctions in the chain ⌈−⌉ ⊣ i ⊣ ⌊−⌋, no instance spared:

galois.py — the sandwich, verified
import math

reals = [i / 7 for i in range(-30, 31)]
ints  = range(-6, 7)
checks = 0
for r in reals:
    for n in ints:
        assert (math.ceil(r) <= n) == (r <= n)    # ceiling  -|  inclusion
        assert (n <= math.floor(r)) == (n <= r)   # inclusion -|  floor
        checks += 2
print(f"ceiling -| inclusion -| floor: verified on {checks} instances")
outputceiling -| inclusion -| floor: verified on 1586 instances

Second, the adjunction that opened the lesson, as running code. fold_map constructs the transpose f̂ from f; we then check the three claims the adjunction makes about it — it is a homomorphism (empty list to unit, concatenation to the operation), and composing with the unit η(x) = [x] recovers f exactly:

free.py — foldMap is the transpose
def fold_map(f, unit, op):
    "the unique monoid hom  List X -> M  extending  f : X -> M"
    def hat(xs):
        acc = unit
        for x in xs:
            acc = op(acc, f(x))
        return acc
    return hat

f   = {'a': 2, 'b': 3}.get
hat = fold_map(f, 0, lambda m, n: m + n)      # target monoid (Z, +, 0)

import itertools
samples = [list(t) for k in range(4)
                   for t in itertools.product('ab', repeat=k)]
assert hat([]) == 0                            # unit to unit
assert all(hat(xs + ys) == hat(xs) + hat(ys)   # concat to +
           for xs in samples for ys in samples)
assert all(hat([x]) == f(x) for x in 'ab')     # hat . eta == f
print("fold_map is a monoid hom; hat.eta == f;",
      "hat(['a','b','a']) =", hat(['a','b','a']))
outputfold_map is a monoid hom; hat.eta == f; hat(['a','b','a']) = 7

Third, the finder. Between finite posets — Lesson 4’s divisibility order and a three-element chain, built as FiniteCategory objects — an adjunction is the Galois biconditional, so the set of all right adjoints of a given monotone map is computable by brute force, in the style of Lessons 4 and 5:

adjoint_finder.py — search for a partner
def monotone_maps(C, D):
    "all monotone maps C -> D between finite posets"
    Cobjs = sorted(C.obj, key=str)
    for values in cartesian(sorted(D.obj, key=str), repeat=len(Cobjs)):
        g = dict(zip(Cobjs, values))
        if all(not arrows_between(C, x, y) or arrows_between(D, g[x], g[y])
               for x in C.obj for y in C.obj):
            yield g

def find_right_adjoints(f, C, D):
    "all monotone g : D -> C  with   f(x) <= y   iff   x <= g(y)"
    return [g for g in monotone_maps(D, C)
            if all(bool(arrows_between(D, f[x], y)) ==
                   bool(arrows_between(C, x, g[y]))
                   for x in C.obj for y in D.obj)]

f = {0: 1, 1: 2, 2: 6}              # Chain {0,1,2}  ->  Div {1,2,3,6}
print("right adjoints of f    :", find_right_adjoints(f, Chain, Div))

f_bad = {1: 1, 2: 1, 3: 1, 6: 1}    # Div -> Chain, constant at 1
print("right adjoints of f_bad:", find_right_adjoints(f_bad, Div, Chain))
outputright adjoints of f : [{1: 0, 2: 1, 3: 0, 6: 2}] right adjoints of f_bad: []

Two lessons in one output. The first search returns a list of length exactly one — the uniqueness of adjoints, witnessed by exhaustion — and the adjoint it finds is g(y) = the largest chain element whose image divides y, the “best under-approximation” pattern again. The second search returns nothing, and RAPL told us it must: f_bad sends the initial object 1 of the divisibility poset to 1 rather than to the chain’s bottom 0, so it fails to preserve a colimit and cannot be a left adjoint. A non-existence theorem, machine-checked.

Going deeper

Quantifiers are adjoints

The most startling entry in the adjunction gallery was left for last. Fix a function f : A → B and consider the two powersets P(A) and P(B), each a poset under inclusion. Preimage f* : P(B) → P(A) is monotone — and it is flanked by two adjoints, both of which every mathematician already uses daily:

∃f ⊣ f* ⊣ ∀f

where ∃f(S) = { f(x) : x ∈ S } is the direct image (“some preimage lies in S”) and ∀f(S) = { b : f−1(b) ⊆ S } (“every preimage lies in S”). The two Galois biconditionals — ∃f(S) ⊆ T ⟺ S ⊆ f*(T) and f*(T) ⊆ S ⟺ T ⊆ ∀f(S) — are, read logically, the introduction and elimination rules for the quantifiers. This observation (Lawvere’s, from the 1960s) is a doorway into categorical logic: the logical quantifiers are not primitive; they are the adjoints of substitution. Exercise 10 verifies both adjunctions exhaustively; the sandwich shape — a right adjoint that is itself a left adjoint — is the ceiling/inclusion/floor pattern again, one floor up.

§6 — Exercises

Eleven problems on adjunctions

Exercise 4 is the summit of the set — the full derivation that unit, counit, and triangles reconstruct the bijection. Everything before it is preparation; everything after it is reward.

Exercise 1warm-up

Write out curry and uncurry in Python for functions given as dictionaries: curry should turn a dict keyed on pairs (x, a) into a dict of dicts, and uncurry should invert it.

Apply them to g(x, a) = x + a on X = A = {0, 1}, confirm the round trip returns the original, and state — carefully, with the sets in the right places — which hom-set bijection your round trip witnesses.

Show solution

curry = lambda h: {x: {a: h[(x, a)] for a in A} for x in X} and uncurry = lambda k: {(x, a): k[x][a] for x in X for a in A}. On g: curry(g) = {0: {0: 0, 1: 1}, 1: {0: 1, 1: 2}}, and uncurry(curry(g)) == g holds.

The round trip witnesses Set(X × A, Y) ≅ Set(X, YA) — with X in the variable slot, A fixed as the adjunction’s parameter, and YA the set of dictionaries keyed on A. It is the unit-level evidence for (−) × A ⊣ (−)A; Exercise 8 upgrades it from “these two functions invert each other on one example” to a counted bijection of entire hom-sets.

Exercise 2warm-up

Prove both halves of the floor/ceiling sandwich from the definitions “⌈r⌉ is the least integer ≥ r” and “⌊r⌋ is the greatest integer ≤ r”:

  1. ⌈r⌉ ≤ n ⟺ r ≤ n for all r ∈ ℝ, n ∈ ℤ.
  2. n ≤ ⌊r⌋ ⟺ n ≤ r for all r ∈ ℝ, n ∈ ℤ.

Then answer in one sentence: why is ceiling the left adjoint and floor the right?

Show solution

(a) (⇒) r ≤ ⌈r⌉ always, so ⌈r⌉ ≤ n gives r ≤ n by transitivity. (⇐) If r ≤ n then n is an integer ≥ r, and ⌈r⌉ is the least such, so ⌈r⌉ ≤ n. (b) is the mirror argument through “greatest integer ≤ r.”

Position in the biconditional decides handedness: in ⌈r⌉ ≤ n ⟺ r ≤ i(n) the ceiling occupies the left slot of f(x) ≤ y ⟺ x ≤ g(y), so ⌈−⌉ ⊣ i; in i(n) ≤ r ⟺ n ≤ ⌊r⌋ the inclusion occupies it, so i ⊣ ⌊−⌋. Note the pattern of the proofs: each “⇒” used a unit-style inequality (r ≤ ⌈r⌉), each “⇐” used an extremality clause — the same division of labor as existence and uniqueness in Lesson 4.

Exercise 3structure

Let f : P → Q and g : Q → P be functions between posets (not assumed monotone) satisfying the Galois biconditional f(x) ≤ y ⟺ x ≤ g(y) for all x, y.

  1. Derive the unit and counit inequalities: x ≤ g(f(x)) and f(g(y)) ≤ y.
  2. Show that f and g are automatically monotone — the biconditional alone forces it.
Show solution

(a) Instantiate the biconditional at y = f(x): the left side f(x) ≤ f(x) holds, so x ≤ g(f(x)). Dually, at x = g(y): the right side holds, so f(g(y)) ≤ y. These are η and ε in a world where a natural transformation is just a family of inequalities (Lesson 3, Exercise 5).

(b) Suppose x ≤ x′. By (a), x′ ≤ g(f(x′)), so x ≤ g(f(x′)) by transitivity — and the biconditional, read right-to-left with y = f(x′), converts this to f(x) ≤ f(x′). Monotonicity of g is the mirror argument. The moral generalizes: an adjunction is such a tightly-coupled structure that properties of one side leak to the other — here, even functoriality came for free.

Exercise 4arrows-only

The summit. Let η : Id ⇒ U∘F and ε : F∘U ⇒ Id satisfy the triangle identities. Define the two candidate transpositions:

φ(f̂) = U f̂ ∘ ηX   for f̂ : F X → Y,     ψ(f) = εY ∘ F f   for f : X → U Y.

Prove φ(ψ(f)) = f and ψ(φ(f̂)) = f̂. Each proof is three steps: expand the definition, slide one arrow across a naturality square (of η in the first proof, of ε in the second), and finish with a triangle identity. Write out which square and which triangle, at which object.

Show solution

First round trip. φ(ψ(f)) = U(εY ∘ F f) ∘ ηX = U εY ∘ U F f ∘ ηX (functoriality of U). Now the naturality square of η at the arrow f : X → U Y says U F f ∘ ηX = ηU Y ∘ f — slide it in: = U εY ∘ ηU Y ∘ f. The second triangle identity, at the object Y, says U εY ∘ ηU Y = idU Y. Hence φ(ψ(f)) = f.

Second round trip. ψ(φ(f̂)) = εY ∘ F(U f̂ ∘ ηX) = εY ∘ F U f̂ ∘ F ηX. Naturality of ε at the arrow f̂ : F X → Y gives εY ∘ F U f̂ = f̂ ∘ εF X: = f̂ ∘ εF X ∘ F ηX, and the first triangle identity at X collapses the tail to idF X. Hence ψ(φ(f̂)) = f̂.

Every tool in the proof was built in an earlier lesson: functor laws (Lesson 2), naturality squares (Lesson 3), and a pair of equations playing exactly the role the “exactly one” clause played in Lessons 4–5. This exercise is the course’s machinery operating as a single machine.

Exercise 5examples

Freeness in action. Let X = {a, b} and let M = (ℕ, +, 0), with f(a) = 2, f(b) = 3.

  1. Compute f̂([a, b, a]).
  2. Prove the uniqueness half of the universal property by hand: any monoid homomorphism h : List X → M with h([x]) = f(x) for all x must equal f̂ on every list. Where do the two homomorphism laws each get used?
Show solution

(a) f̂([a, b, a]) = f(a) + f(b) + f(a) = 2 + 3 + 2 = 7 — as §5’s run printed.

(b) Any list factors as a concatenation of singletons: [x₁, …, xₙ] = [x₁] ⧺ ⋯ ⧺ [xₙ]. Since h preserves the operation, h([x₁, …, xₙ]) = h([x₁]) + ⋯ + h([xₙ]) = f(x₁) + ⋯ + f(xₙ) = f̂([x₁, …, xₙ]); and the empty list is the unit, so preservation of units forces h([]) = 0, covering the base case. Operation-preservation handled every list of length ≥ 1; unit-preservation handled length 0; no room was left anywhere.

This is what “no relations beyond the forced ones” buys: the generators generate, so agreement on generators plus the homomorphism laws leaves zero freedom. It is also why the adjoint is unique — any two candidate free monoids on X receive mutually inverse homomorphisms by exactly this argument, Lesson 4’s uniqueness proof wearing a new hat.

Exercise 6structure

The product category 𝒞 × 𝒞 has pairs of objects as objects and pairs of arrows as arrows, composed slotwise; the diagonal functor Δ sends X to (X, X) and f to (f, f).

  1. Describe the hom-set (𝒞 × 𝒞)(Δ X, (A, B)) and show that Lesson 4’s product universal property is exactly the statement (𝒞 × 𝒞)(Δ X, (A, B)) ≅ 𝒞(X, A × B) — i.e. Δ ⊣ ×.
  2. Dualize to show + ⊣ Δ, and name the counit of Δ ⊣ × and the unit of + ⊣ Δ — both are old friends.
Show solution

(a) An arrow Δ X → (A, B) is a pair (f, g) with f : X → A and g : X → B — nothing more. Lesson 4’s product says exactly that such pairs correspond bijectively (and naturally — the pairing construction respects composition) to single arrows X → A × B, via (f, g) ↔ ⟨f, g⟩. That bijection is the adjunction Δ ⊣ ×.

(b) Reversing arrows: 𝒞(A + B, X) ≅ (𝒞 × 𝒞)((A, B), Δ X) — a map out of the coproduct is a pair of maps — putting + in the left slot: + ⊣ Δ ⊣ ×, a sandwich with Δ as the bread, just like the inclusion between ceiling and floor. The counit of Δ ⊣ × at (A, B) is the pair of projections (π₁, π₂) : (A×B, A×B) → (A, B); the unit of + ⊣ Δ is the pair of injections. Lesson 4’s cast of characters were unit and counit all along — and replacing Δ by Lesson 5’s constant-diagram functor makes every limit a right adjoint and every colimit a left one.

Exercise 7think

A two-line non-existence theorem. Using RAPL’s dual — left adjoints preserve colimits, in particular initial objects — prove that the forgetful functor U : Grp → Set has no right adjoint.

(You will need Lesson 4’s inventory: the initial object of Grp is the trivial group; the initial object of Set is the empty set.)

Show solution

If U had a right adjoint, U would be a left adjoint, and would therefore preserve initial objects. But U sends the initial object of Grp — the trivial group — to a one-element set, and the initial object of Set is the empty set. One-element sets are not initial (they receive many maps, and Lesson 4, Exercise 1 counted them), so U does not preserve the initial object, and no right adjoint exists. ∎

Notice the asymmetry with the other flank: U does have a left adjoint (free groups), which is why it preserves all limits — products, kernels-as-equalizers, pullbacks. The two adjoint questions about a single functor are genuinely independent, and RAPL turns each into a checklist of concrete preservation tests. §5’s f_bad is this exact argument run by machine in a six-element universe.

Exercise 8code

Upgrade Exercise 1 to a census. For X = A = {0, 1} and Y = {0, 1, 2}, enumerate all functions X × A → Y, curry each one, and verify that currying is a bijection onto the functions X → YA — by checking injectivity and comparing counts against |Y||X|·|A| and (|Y||A|)|X|.

Before running: why must those two counts be equal, and what law of arithmetic is the adjunction reproving?

Show solution

The counts are 3⁴ = 81 on both sides, and they must agree because currying is a bijection — the adjunction, restricted to finite sets and counted, is the exponent law yx·a = (ya)x. High-school algebra is the decategorified shadow of (−) × A ⊣ (−)A.

census.py
from itertools import product as cartesian

X, A, Y = range(2), range(2), range(3)
pairs = [(x, a) for x in X for a in A]
hs = [dict(zip(pairs, vs)) for vs in cartesian(Y, repeat=len(pairs))]

curry  = lambda h: tuple(tuple(h[(x, a)] for a in A) for x in X)
images = {curry(h) for h in hs}

print("|Hom(XxA,Y)| =", len(hs), " distinct curried =", len(images),
      " (|Y|^|A|)^|X| =", (len(Y) ** len(A)) ** len(X))
output|Hom(XxA,Y)| = 81 distinct curried = 81 (|Y|^|A|)^|X| = 81

81 inputs, 81 distinct curried outputs (injectivity), 81 possible targets: injective + equal finite cardinalities = bijection. The same three-number certificate would verify the bijection for any finite X, A, Y.

Exercise 9code

Dualize the finder, in the tradition of Lessons 4 and 5: write find_left_adjoints(g, D, C), which searches for all monotone f : C → D satisfying the biconditional against a given g : D → C.

Run it on the adjoint that §5 discovered, g = {1:0, 2:1, 3:0, 6:2}. Predict the output first: how long should the list be, and what should be in it?

Show solution

Prediction: adjoints determine each other, so the list should have length exactly one, containing the original f = {0:1, 1:2, 2:6} — the finder run in reverse should return us to our starting point.

reverse.py
def find_left_adjoints(g, D, C):
    "all monotone f : C -> D  with   f(x) <= y   iff   x <= g(y)"
    return [f for f in monotone_maps(C, D)
            if all(bool(arrows_between(D, f[x], y)) ==
                   bool(arrows_between(C, x, g[y]))
                   for x in C.obj for y in D.obj)]

Confirmed: exactly one left adjoint, and it is the original f. The biconditional is symmetric in the data it constrains, so each partner pins the other down — uniqueness of adjoints, demonstrated in both directions by the same search. Note what the dualization changed: only which side of the biconditional is quantified over candidates; the condition itself is untouched. Duality economizes even on code.

Exercise 10code · new

Verify the quantifier sandwich by exhaustion. Take A = {1, 2, 3}, B = {a, b}, and f(1) = f(2) = a, f(3) = b. Implement ∃f, f*, and ∀f, and check both Galois biconditionals over all pairs of subsets — 8 × 4 pairs, two biconditionals each.

One subtlety to handle before running: what should ∀f do with an element of B that has no preimages at all?

Show solution

An element with empty preimage satisfies “every preimage lies in S” vacuously, so it belongs to ∀f(S) for every S — the code’s all(...) over an empty iterable returns True, which is the right answer for the same vacuous reason the empty function made ∅ initial in Lesson 4.

quantifiers.py
from itertools import combinations

A = {1, 2, 3}; B = {'a', 'b'}
fpt = {1: 'a', 2: 'a', 3: 'b'}
f = lambda x: fpt[x]

def subsets(S):
    S = list(S)
    return [set(c) for r in range(len(S)+1) for c in combinations(S, r)]

E   = lambda S: {f(x) for x in S}                     # ∃f : direct image
pre = lambda T: {x for x in A if f(x) in T}           # f* : preimage
All = lambda S: {b for b in B                         # ∀f
                 if all(x in S for x in A if f(x) == b)}

n = 0
for S in subsets(A):
    for T in subsets(B):
        assert (E(S) <= T) == (S <= pre(T))     # ∃f  -|  f*
        assert (pre(T) <= S) == (T <= All(S))   # f*  -|  ∀f
        n += 2
print(f"quantifier sandwich verified on {n} biconditionals")

Sixty-four checks, zero exceptions. Reading the units: S ⊆ f*(∃f S) says “everything in S maps into the image of S” — a tautology, which is what units of adjunctions usually are once decoded. Quantifiers, images, preimages: the logic you already knew, filed under Lesson 6.

Exercise 11arrows-only · new

Adjunctions compose. Given F ⊣ U between 𝒞 and 𝒟, and F′ ⊣ U′ between 𝒟 and ℰ, show that F′ ∘ F ⊣ U ∘ U′ — and note carefully which functor ends up on which side.

Show solution

Chain the two bijections: ℰ(F′F X, Z) ≅ 𝒟(F X, U′Z) ≅ 𝒞(X, U U′ Z) — the first by F′ ⊣ U′ at the pair (F X, Z), the second by F ⊣ U at (X, U′Z). A composite of bijections is a bijection, and each stage is natural in both slots, so the composite is too. Note the order: the left adjoints compose as F′ ∘ F (apply F first) while the right adjoints compose as U ∘ U′ (apply U′ first) — reversed, socks-and-shoes yet again.

Instances are everywhere once you look: free monoid on a set factors as “free semigroup, then adjoin a unit”; and in the quantifier sandwich, ∃ along a composite function is the composite of the ∃’s — image-of-image, the covariant powerset functor law of Lesson 2’s new Exercise 11, now recognized as composed left adjoints.

Recap

What to carry forward

  1. An adjunction F ⊣ U is a bijection 𝒟(F X, Y) ≅ 𝒞(X, U Y), natural in both variables — a perfect exchange rate between two kinds of arrow. The left adjoint sits on the left of the hom-set.
  2. Equivalently: a unit η : Id ⇒ UF and counit ε : FU ⇒ Id satisfying the two triangle identities. Transposition is U(−) ∘ η one way and ε ∘ F(−) the other.
  3. The gallery: free ⊣ forgetful (foldMap), currying (−) × A ⊣ (−)A with eval as counit, Galois connections in posets (⌈−⌉ ⊣ i ⊣ ⌊−⌋), and + ⊣ Δ ⊣ × — limits and colimits are adjoints of diagonal functors.
  4. RAPL: right adjoints preserve limits, left adjoints preserve colimits — and the contrapositive proves non-existence of adjoints in two lines.
  5. Adjoints are unique up to natural isomorphism, and in finite posets they are literally findable by search — with the found list always of length one or zero.
